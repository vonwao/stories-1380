# The Last Human Lane

I worked lane one at the Lantana toll plaza, milepost eighty-eight on Florida's Turnpike, for twenty-nine years. For the last six of those it was the only cash lane left between Miami and Fort Pierce. Everything else went SunPass. The transponder lanes don't have a gate or a light or a person; they have a camera and a little beep you can't hear over your own radio, and they take a car every two seconds. My lane took a car about every twenty-five, and that was when I was quick and the driver had the toll ready, which they mostly didn't.

The cash toll was three dollars even. I want that on the record, because it matters later. Three dollars, lane one, far right, the lane with the actual human being in it. There's a sign half a mile out that says CASH, and for years the only people who used it were tourists, drivers with dead transponder batteries, and a man named Hector who didn't believe in accounts of any kind.

Then, on a Tuesday in February, a woman in a gray Camry waited in my line behind two other cars. I could see the SunPass on her windshield, mounted right where it's supposed to be. She could have rolled through lane four without slowing down. Instead she handed me three ones and said, "I lost the baby in March. Nobody at work knows."

The light turned green. She drove off. I held out the receipt and she didn't take it.

By May there was a line at my lane most mornings while the SunPass lanes ran empty on either side of me, which if you've driven this state you know is against the laws of Florida and possibly nature. They came with exact change, mostly. One sentence each. A man in a lawn-service truck: "I can't read. My boy does the invoices and thinks I check them." A kid maybe eighteen: "I got into FSU and I'm not going, and I haven't told my mother." An old man in a Buick with the air conditioning roaring: "I liked the hurricane. Both days of it. Best week of the marriage in years." A woman in a Publix uniform who said, "I'm the one who hit the mailbox," paid her three dollars, and laughed the whole way through the light.

Nobody told them the rules. The rules just showed up, the way a path shows up across a lawn. Exact change if you could manage it. One true thing, said once. They didn't want answers, and I learned fast not to offer any. What I said was, "Thank you. Watch the merge." That's a real warning, by the way. The lanes come back together fast past the plaza, and people pulling away from a thing like that don't always check their mirrors.

I don't know how it started, and the internet doesn't either, because the internet tried. A news van parked on the shoulder for two days in June. While the camera was up, everybody went through SunPass like normal citizens, and the reporter did her stand-up in front of an empty cash lane and called the whole thing a rumor. The van left on a Thursday. Friday morning the line was eleven cars deep.

What I think, for whatever a toll collector's theory is worth, is this. Every other place you can say a true thing keeps a copy. The therapist has notes. The priest has a memory and a policy. The phone in your pocket is the phone in your pocket. My lane had a man with a name tag nobody read, no recorder, nowhere to put anything you handed me except the cash drawer, and a light that turned green in nine seconds whether you cried or not. They weren't paying the toll. The toll was the cover charge. Three dollars to be heard by somebody who couldn't do one single thing about it.

That's not an arrangement the Turnpike has a code for. What it has codes for is throughput.

The letter came from the district office in Pompano Beach in October, and it was polite the way those letters are polite. Lane one processed an average of 142 vehicles per hour against 1,160 for an electronic lane. Wait times at the Lantana plaza exceeded targets. The cash lane would be decommissioned effective January fifth, converted to open-road tolling, and I was thanked for twenty-nine years of service and offered a position at the call center in Boca, answering phones about billing disputes. Nobody was being cruel. There was just nothing in the lane's defense you could type into the field where the numbers go.

A sign went up sixty days out, white on orange. CASH LANE CLOSES JAN 5. After that the line got longer, and quieter.

On the last day they came from before sunrise until the end of my shift, and I kept what they said the way I kept all of it, which is the only way I had. Nowhere. Near the end the gray Camry came through again. Same woman. She handed me three ones and didn't say anything, and the light went green, and she didn't move. Behind her, eleven cars, and nobody honked.

"Your turn," she said.

So I said it, out the window of my own booth, to a stranger in a Camry. "I have a daughter in Jacksonville I haven't spoken to in six years, and I don't remember anymore which one of us stopped calling."

She nodded. She took the receipt. She watched the merge.

They tore the booth out in a week. There's a gantry there now, a steel arch with cameras on it, and the cars go under it at eighty without touching their brakes. In February a bill came in the mail, TOLL-BY-PLATE, three dollars and nine cents, with a photograph of the back of my car. Because yes, I drive through there. Most Sundays now, on the way up to Jacksonville and back.

The camera gets my plate every time. It has never once gotten what I say.

I watch the merge.
