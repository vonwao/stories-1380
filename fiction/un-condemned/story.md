# Un-Condemned

My signature is on two hundred and eleven demolition orders. I counted them in January, in the records room on Blue Heron Boulevard, because the clerk still lets me behind the counter, and because I wanted the number exact before I decided what to do about it.

For thirty years I inspected buildings for the City of Riviera Beach, Florida. The last eleven of those I was the chief building official, which means the orders stopped crossing my desk and started coming from it. I retired in two thousand nineteen with a pension, a plaque, and a reputation for being exact, which in this trade is the only reputation there is.

Here is what people get wrong about condemnation. They think a condemned house is a dangerous house. Sometimes it is. But unsafe structure, in the code, is an arithmetic term. Section one-sixteen says a building is unsafe when the cost to repair it exceeds fifty percent of its assessed value. Assessed value on West Thirtieth Street runs low, because the county says so, and repair estimates run high, because the contractors writing them are usually bidding on the demolition too. So a house with a soft roof and an old panel box, on a street the county appraises at next to nothing, fails the arithmetic every time. The arithmetic was never asked whether anybody lives there.

I wrote some of that arithmetic. I want that stated early, in my own words, so nobody catches me at it later.

Some of the two hundred and eleven needed to go. A duplex on Avenue E with the floor joists eaten to lace. A house with a gas line repaired with a garden hose. But I can also tell you about the house on Avenue H that I condemned in nineteen ninety-four while the owner was at Saint Mary's recovering from a stroke, with the notice taped to a door she could not come home to read. The code allowed it. I know it allowed it, because when she appealed, I am the one who explained, politely, that it did. The lot is still vacant. It has been vacant for thirty-one years, which is longer than the house got to be a house.

That is the part nobody follows out to the end. The city demolishes for about eleven thousand dollars and records the eleven thousand as a lien on the land. The family that could not afford a roof now owns a bare lot they cannot afford either, and in four or five years it goes to tax deed sale, and somebody from out of county buys it for the cost of the lien. Since nineteen ninety the city has demolished three hundred and six houses. In February I drove every street and counted the lots still sitting empty. One hundred and seventy-one. Nobody was protected by a hundred and seventy-one rectangles of weeds. They were just produced, the way a process produces what it produces.

So this is what I do with my retirement. The special magistrate's agenda comes out the first of every month, and I read it the way other retired men read box scores. When I find an address with a family still in it, I knock.

In February I knocked at fourteen-twenty West Thirtieth Street. Concrete block, built nineteen fifty-eight, a roof that hurricane Wilma loosened in two thousand five and nobody with a license had touched since. Carline Estimé answered the door with the orange notice in her hand, because she had taken it down off the plywood so her kids would stop reading it. Her grandmother bought that house in nineteen seventy-one, cleaned rooms at the old hotel on Singer Island to pay for it, and died in it two years ago, in her own bed, which is the good way. The estate was never probated, because probate costs money. So the owner of record is a dead woman, and her granddaughter lives there with two children, eleven and seven, and forty-five days on a notice, and a repair estimate of sixty-two thousand dollars against an assessed value of fifty-eight. You can finish that math at the kitchen table, which is what she was doing when I knocked.

She thought I was from the city. That is fair. I used to be the city.

I told her three things. First, that an active repair permit, with an inspection passed every ninety days, stays a demolition order in this city. That sentence is in the code because I put it there, in nineteen ninety-six, after the Avenue H house. The commission adopted it at eleven-forty at night without discussion. Nobody fights over a sentence with no money in it.

Second, that sixty-two thousand was a teardown number, written by a man who wanted the teardown. The roof was a nine-thousand-dollar roof if a church crew swung the hammers and a retired man supervised for free. The panel box, a Federal Pacific, the kind that lets a short cook in the wall, was eighteen hundred more. The fascia was paint and lumber. I have spent thirty years pricing what it costs to kill a house. I know to the dollar what it costs to keep one alive.

Third, that the city had mailed her notice addressed to occupant. When the owner of record is deceased, chapter one-sixty-two requires notice to the estate by publication. They never published. I know that shortcut. I taught that shortcut. In nineteen ninety-eight I stood in a hallway with a new inspector named Ray and told him occupant covers it, nobody ever checks. Ray runs the department now. He is the best inspector I ever trained, and for twenty-six years he was my friend, and I want both halves of that sentence on the record.

The hearing was the ninth of April, a Thursday, nine in the morning, council chambers. The special magistrate is a real estate attorney from North Palm who is courteous to everyone and bound to the record. Ray testified first, and he was right about almost everything. The decking over the kitchen was soft. The panel was a hazard. He had photographs, and he read that building exactly the way I taught him to read buildings, and he was not lying and he was not cruel. If Carline's daughter had been standing in that kitchen in the wrong storm, the roof would not have asked whose side anyone was on. I fixed that house because Ray was right about it. I want that on the record too.

Then it was my turn, as the owner's authorized representative, and I asked him three questions. Whether the owner of record was deceased. Whether the estate had been noticed by publication, as the statute requires. And whether the roof permit at that address had passed its first inspection on the second of April. No, sir. No, sir. Yes, sir. The magistrate said the word deficient once, quietly, the way you would say it about a weld, vacated the order, and called the next item. Eleven minutes, start to finish.

Ray packed his folder without looking at me. In the parking lot he shook my hand the way you shake hands with the public.

For twenty-six years we had breakfast every Saturday at the Sunrise on Broadway. Eggs over medium for him, scrambled for me, and the kind of talk two inspectors have, which from the outside sounds like complaining and from the inside is the whole friendship. The Saturday after the hearing I sat in our booth until nine-thirty. He did not come. He has not come since, and I understand it, and that is the worst part, that I understand it. I did not catch him doing something wrong. I caught him doing something I taught him, under oath, in a room where his own inspectors could watch. A man might forgive being beaten. I do not know one who forgives being used as the proof.

The church crew came on the Saturdays Ray stopped owning. Eight men from New Bethel on West Thirty-Fifth, and Carline's son carrying shingles up to the eave two at a time, very serious about it. I checked the stapling pattern myself, every course. The panel passed on the first try. The inspector who passed it is twenty-eight years old and has no idea who I am, and I find that I prefer it.

I do not tell myself any of this evens out. One house back against two hundred and eleven down is not arithmetic anyone respects. The new agenda came out on the first of the month with four more addresses on it, and I am sixty-eight, and the city has started mailing its notices correctly now, and I hear they are drafting an amendment to my permit sentence. They are within their rights. It is their code now. But I will say the one thing I believe, the thing Ray would argue with me about if Ray were still speaking to me. A code that can put a family on the street by long division was never about safety. We told ourselves the houses were the danger. Mostly the houses were just poor.

In October, Carline had me to dinner. Rice and beans, stewed chicken, the kids loud in the good way. From her porch you can see two empty lots where my signature did its work, and her house sits between them with every light on. Her daughter spread her homework on the kitchen table, under the new panel, under the new roof. That roof is rated for a hundred and sixty miles an hour. It will outlast me. It will outlast the lien that is no longer on it. And if you drive down West Thirtieth slow, after dark, the house looks like what it is now, which is not unsafe, and not condemned. Lived in.
