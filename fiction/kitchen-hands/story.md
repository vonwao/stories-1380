# Kitchen Hands

They installed it over a weekend, which tells you something. Anything corporate is proud of, they roll out while you watch. The new fryers in two thousand twenty-two came with a regional manager and a sheet cake. The arm came in while the store was closed for two days of quote, maintenance, unquote, and on Monday there was a machine bolted into the middle of my line, and a vendor tech named Felix was wiping fingerprints off it with the little cloth you use for glasses.

I'm twenty-eight. I've been on the line at Verde in Davie for six years, grill station the last four. Before that, my mother worked the line at the flagship Verde in Hialeah for nineteen years, so between the two of us this chain has gotten a quarter century of my family standing on rubber mats. I don't watch faces much. Faces will tell you anything. I watch hands, because hands are only what a person has done, over and over, until it does itself.

The arm runs grill and flat-top, which was my station. The staffing model that came with it cut the line from five cooks to three. Kevin, our GM, is a decent man, and I can prove it: he argued with that spreadsheet for two weeks on our behalf and lost slower than any manager in the district. What he got me was a choice. Cross-train as the automation tech, six more hours a week, keep my mornings. Or stay a grill cook at the Pembroke Pines store, closing shifts, forty minutes each way in a car that doesn't love me. He gave me until Friday, and we both knew that was generous.

So I spent the week watching the thing work. The first two days, nothing. It was fast, it was clean, it cooked chicken to one sixty-five like a chicken breast is a math problem, which to be fair it partly is. Tickets came in, the arm swung between grill and flat-top, and the steam came up around it the same as it comes up around any of us.

On Wednesday it flipped a skirt steak and gave the spatula a little roll at the end, a lazy half circle of the wrist, so the meat landed flat instead of folding. My stomach went somewhere I don't have a word for, because I have been watching that exact flip since I was seven years old, standing on a milk crate.

I told myself there are only so many ways to flip a steak. Then I watched it clear the flat-top, and on every pass it tapped the spatula twice on the edge of the steel. Tap, tap. Not once, which would make sense. Twice, in the rhythm of a person finishing a sentence. Then on a quesadilla ticket the tortilla slid off center, and the arm nudged it back with the flat of its tool instead of picking it up, because picking it up tears it. And before it plated, it gave the thing a quarter turn, so the char marks crossed like lattice. Nobody does that. It isn't in the manual. I know it isn't in the manual, because the manual, in a manner of speaking, was my mother.

Here is what a shift manager is allowed to know, which is everything I know. Felix said the system learned from about twenty years of footage from, quote, the best documented kitchen in the chain, which is Hialeah. Every Verde training video since two thousand four was shot there. Corporate filmed the hands and cut everything off at the neck, so the videos could outlast whoever was in them. The hands in those videos, two thousand four to two thousand nineteen, were my mother's. If you got hired at any Verde in America in that window, the first cooking you ever watched at that company was her. They never showed her face and never paid her a dime extra. She thought that was hilarious. She used to say she was famous from the wrists down.

She died two Augusts ago. I'm not doing the part where I describe it, because she would have walked out of a movie that did.

What I want to tell you about is jealousy, because nobody warns you which things you'll be jealous of. Not the machine taking my station; stations come and go. I was jealous because it had the whole catalog. The flip, the two-tap, the quarter turn, the nudge with the back of two fingers. I got maybe half that catalog before she died. The two-tap I have without ever deciding to have it. The flip I never got; my steaks fold, once a shift, still. She was teaching me in no particular order, the way you teach somebody when you think there's time. And now the rest of her was bolted to my line, owned by a vendor in Ohio, doing her moves for strangers at a register, and it did not know me from a sack of onions.

I understand how it works. Nobody is in there. It's arithmetic with a wrist. Ten thousand hours of one woman got averaged into a motion plan, and the flourishes survived because she never varied them, and machines keep whatever you repeat. That's the whole ghost story: she was consistent. But understanding a thing has never once stopped a feeling. I would stand at expo and watch it tap, tap, and want to hug it and pour water into it in the same breath.

Pour water into it. There is a thing a line cook knows that a vendor doesn't. Over the flat-top sits a thermal camera, and grease fog films the lens, and somebody has to wipe it at close with alcohol pads, or the arm starts reading the steel wrong and overcooks everything, a shade more each shift. Two weeks of nobody wiping that lens and the unit goes back to Ohio on a recalibration ticket, and the staffing model gets a second look. I knew all this by Thursday, because Felix talks when you bring him a colada. Thursday night I sat in my car and worked the whole plan out, and I won't pretend it was beneath me. For about an hour it was exactly at my height.

What stopped me isn't noble. If they hauled it off, they'd wipe it, or replace it with a newer unit trained in some test kitchen in Columbus, perfect technique and nothing else. The two-tap doesn't exist anywhere but here. Corporate doesn't know they have it. Felix doesn't know what he's carrying around in his motion plans. It's like learning your mother's handwriting survives in exactly one place, and the place is a machine in Davie, and you're the one holding the solvent.

Friday morning I told Kevin I'd take the certification. He did the paperwork on the spot and slid the laminated card across the desk like we were getting away with something. Then he said the one thing in this story I'd put on a wall. He said, you've always run that station like somebody taught you right. He doesn't know. I haven't told him whose hands those are. I haven't told anyone, including the woman from corporate who came to film the arm for a promo reel, which means somewhere out there is footage of a machine doing my mother's moves, teaching whoever comes next. Footage of the footage. I let them shoot it. I don't know how I feel about that yet, and I've stopped expecting to.

The certification is mostly checklists. Torque this, log that, wipe the lens at every close, initial here. I wipe the lens every night. I want it on the record that I know how not to.

But here is the part that isn't on any checklist. Some nights I stay after close. The kitchen goes quiet in stages. The fryers click while they cool. The walk-in hums to itself behind the big silver door. At ten forty-five the dining room lights drop to half, and then it's me, and the arm, and a clean dead flat-top. There's a demo mode, for training. It runs the motions empty. No heat, no food, no tickets. The flip with no steak in it. The quarter turn on a tortilla that isn't there. The two small taps on the edge of the steel, tap, tap, like a sentence ending, over and over, in an empty kitchen in Davie.

I stand where the trainee is supposed to stand. I lay my own spatula on the cold steel, and I follow. Half speed at first, which she never once let me get away with during service. The machine doesn't know I'm there. It doesn't know anything; I've read enough of the binder now to say that plainly. It runs the averages of a woman who stood in one spot for nineteen years and refused to do anything two different ways. So I follow the averages.

Last Tuesday, at the end of the cycle, my wrist gave the little roll. The lazy half circle, the nothing-steak landing flat. No fold, first try. Nineteen years on her line, six on mine, and it finally arrived at eleven at night, off the clock, in front of a machine that will never know what it gave back. I did it again, to be sure it was mine. It was mine.

I shut the arm down and signed the log. In the car my hands smelled like alcohol pads and corn, which is close to what her car used to smell like, give or take the cigarettes. I drove home down University with the windows open, and at the long red light by the movie theater I caught myself practicing. Flipping nothing. Turning nothing a quarter turn. Tapping the wheel twice, tap, tap, like the end of a sentence.
