# The Mango Tree

There is a mango tree on Spruce Street, in the Northwood neighborhood of West Palm Beach, that grows out of two properties at once. The trunk sits on the line. Not near the line. On it. I have paid for two surveys that say so, and Ernesto Quintana paid for one of his own in between, and all three agree, which is the only thing the two sides of that fence ever agreed on in thirty-four years. The tree is a Haden, planted by somebody sensible who died before either of us bought in, and it has never once cared.

People ask me how it started, the way you ask the survivor of something.

In June of nineteen ninety-one, at a block party on the Quintana side, I ate a mango off that tree and told a woman I was trying to impress that it was stringy. Ernesto was behind me with the lighter fluid for the grill. He heard all of it.

I have had thirty-four years to come up with a better beginning than that, and I never did, because it was true.

A Haden is a beautiful fruit, red and gold, big as a softball, and the ones off our tree were stringy every summer, and Ernesto knew it, and that is exactly why it could not be forgiven. If I had insulted his car, he would have let it go.

People say a feud costs you. I can give you the figure. Two surveys, eleven hundred and seventy dollars. My half of the fence, twelve hundred even. The fence stops short of the trunk on both sides, so the tree stands in the one gap in the wall, like a door neither of us would use. Code fines, six hundred and forty. Two letters from a lawyer about whose half-grown mangoes were rotting on whose grass, three hundred and twenty. And eighty-six dollars for a pair of glasses, which I will get to. All told, three thousand four hundred and sixteen dollars over thirty-four years, which comes to about a hundred a year, which is cheaper than cable, and I watched it more.

The glasses were two thousand sixteen. I was pressure washing my side of the fence. Ernesto got up on a paint bucket on his side to inform me that my spray was crossing the line, and while he was up there, I turned around to answer him with the wand still in my hand. They will tell you on Spruce Street that I knocked a seventy-year-old man off a bucket with a pressure washer. I want it stated plainly that the bucket held, the man held, and only the glasses came off, into his crotons. I mailed him a check with UNDER PROTEST on the memo line, and he cashed it the same day, and I respected that.

People don't understand that the feud had rules. In thirty-four years, neither one of us ever set a shoe across that line. Ernesto had a pole picker, a wire basket with a flour-sack bag on a ten-foot pole, and come June he would stand flat-footed on his own grass and strip every mango hanging over his side, quick as dealing cards. So I bought a fourteen-foot picker from a catalog. He extended his with PVC pipe and a hose clamp. By the end, the two of us could pick the entire crown of that tree without trespassing an inch.

Every May the tree threw off half its crop, little green ones the size of a fist. The June drop, growers call it, even in Florida, where it happens in May. We raked our own sides. That took the lawyer. And every June we raced for the keepers, because of the rats. Roof rats hollow out a ripe mango overnight and leave the skin hanging on the stem like a little paper lantern, and whoever picked late was feeding them. So nobody picked late. That tree got stripped cleaner than a commercial grove for thirty years. The feud was the best thing that ever happened to it.

In two thousand eleven I went up the ladder to clean my gutters and came down the fast way, and broke my hip. The ambulance made it in four minutes, because Ernesto called it in before I was done hitting the ground, which means he was watching me clean my gutters, which means he was watching, period. While I was in rehab, somebody watered my ixora. And when I got home, there was a code complaint waiting, about a ladder left lying against a shared fence. I understood it completely. Things were back to normal. It is the most reassuring piece of mail I have ever gotten.

Two weeks before I fell, Ernesto had told me over the fence, in Spanish, that my top rung was rusted. I took it as a provocation.

Ernesto died on the third of March, at Saint Mary's, of the heart that everybody including Ernesto knew about and nobody could tell him anything. I went to the funeral and stood in the back and counted the flower arrangements, eleven, and his daughter Ileana picked me out anyway. Her father must have been pointing me out to her for her whole life, the way you point out a canal you don't swim in.

In April, the grass at the empty house got long, and I called code enforcement on it. I'll say that plainly, because there is no version that sounds better. Gave my name, like always. The man on the phone told me the owner was deceased, and I said I knew that, and then I sat there holding the phone like a man who has dialed his own number. Then I went over and mowed the swale myself. Only the strip the city measures. I told myself it was property values.

My coffee chair faces the side window. I would have sworn it faced the breeze. It faces his kitchen. For thirty-four years his light came on at six and mine came on at six-thirty, and I drank my coffee watching a man do nothing wrong yet. In April I was still in that chair every morning, watching a dark window, with my list ready. I left my utility trailer in the front yard for nine days, to see. Nothing came. You can do whatever you want, it turns out, once nobody is keeping track of you.

Ileana started coming on Saturdays to empty the house, and one Saturday she handed me an accordion folder. My name was on the tab in marker. "It was in his desk," she said. "It's mostly you." Inside was the carbon of his survey, and copies of every complaint, both directions, and a photocopy of my eighty-six dollar check, both sides. And margins. On the complaint from two thousand eleven, next to the date, in pencil, it says, told him about the rung.

She asked if I wanted to keep it. I said somebody has to keep the records. She said, "I'm not doing this, you know. Whatever this was. I don't have one of these for you, is what I'm saying." That's fair. You can't leave a feud to somebody. It only ever fit the two people it was made for.

Now the part I can't make anything of.

The tree bloomed in January, both sides, like every year. The May drop came, and every green one of it dropped on his side. In June the keepers sized up, and I counted from my driveway with my coffee. Eighty-one mangoes. Eighty-one of them hanging over the dead man's side of the line. My branches had bloomed and set nothing, not one fruit, and the branches over Ernesto's grass hung so heavy his daughter would have needed to prop them. I walked the line twice with the survey in my hand. Trees have their years, and nobody who grows mangoes will swear to you what a tree is going to do, and that tree never did that before, and it did it the year he died. I wrote it in the folder, because somebody keeps the records.

The fruit turned the third week of June. Ileana doesn't know mangoes. She let them hang. On the Monday I saw the first lantern, a hollowed-out skin turning on its stem. On the Tuesday morning, I took the fourteen-foot picker and walked around the fence and through his gate, onto Quintana grass, for the first time since June of nineteen ninety-one.

She came out the kitchen door and stood there with her arms crossed, and she stood exactly like him.

"The rats are beating you," I said. "Get a bag."

We picked for two hours. I worked the pole and she caught. You pick a mango with a little push upward, so the stem lets go, and the sap jumps when it snaps, and the sap burns skin, so you learn to hold the basket away from your face. I taught her that. Her father would have taught her that, if anybody had ever asked him anything.

Eighty-one mangoes came off the tree. We left the rats nothing. She kept forty and sent forty-one home with me over my objection, in two grocery bags. Hers. His. Off his side, every one.

I left the pole picker leaning inside her gate. She will need it next May for the drop, and next June for the crop. She doesn't know that yet. I expect I will have to be the one who tells her.

That night I ate one over the sink, the way you have to eat a Haden, peel back, both hands, no dignity in it. It was stringy. Of course it was stringy. I ate it down to the seed anyway, standing at the side window, looking at his dark kitchen. Then I ate another one.
