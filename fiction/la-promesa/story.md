# La Promesa

We buried my mother on a Saturday morning at Our Lady of Mercy, and by two in the afternoon the house in Westchester was full of people eating her food without her. Somebody's pasticho came out of the freezer, and the living room filled with men watching baseball with the sound off.

I was at the sink washing cups when my tía Belén dried her hands on a towel and said, in Spanish, in the voice she uses for dentist appointments, "You know she still owed the promesa. To José Gregorio. It passes to you now, mijo."

I said, "Okay, Tía," the way you say okay to old ladies at funerals. She looked at me a second longer than necessary, went off to fight about the flan, and I didn't think of it again for twenty-nine days.

I fix air conditioners for a living. My own truck, my own customers, my own way of laying out my tools. On the twenty-sixth of June I came out of a job on Coral Way and my tape measure was not in its pocket on my belt. A Stanley, twenty-five foot. One pound, one ounce. The weight is printed on the box. I know that now for a reason.

A tape measure is nothing. It falls behind a compressor. I bought another one.

On the twenty-sixth of July, my father's belt buckle disappeared off my dresser. Brass, from Venezuela, a horse on it, the one thing of his I kept. In the morning the belt lay there flat with no buckle, like a snake with no head. I accused the neighbor's dog, who has never been inside my house. Maritza watched me empty every drawer twice and said nothing, which after eighteen years of marriage is its own language.

In August it was the spare key to my mother's house, gone while the key ring sat closed in the dish by the door. In September, my good multimeter, out of a locked truck. One pound, two ounces. Each of these has an explanation if you want one. Keys fall. Trucks get popped for less. And I know how the brain works. I have customers who swear their thermostat is haunted, and it is never haunted, it is always the batteries.

But invoices have dates. One night I sat down with a beer and went through the calendar, and everything had gone missing on a twenty-sixth. Four for four. I sat there a long time telling myself batteries, batteries, and not believing me.

So I went to see Tía Belén. She poured the coffee before I finished my first sentence.

"El veintiséis," she said. "Claro. That's his day."

Then she told it plain, the way you tell weather. Nineteen eighty-three, La Victoria. I was eight months old, with a fever the clinic couldn't bring down. A hundred and five, then worse. The third night, the doctor stopped saying things to my mother and started saying them only to my aunt, and my mother got down on the tile, on her knees, in front of the little picture of José Gregorio Hernández — the doctor saint, the man in the black suit and hat from my grandmother's hallway — and made the promesa out loud. His weight in candle wax, carried to Isnotú, where the saint was born, before the boy turned one.

"Out loud," Tía said. "You don't make a promesa in your head, mijo. He has to hear it, and the family has to hear it, so somebody can collect if you die first." The fever broke before morning. On the way out, my mother made the nurses weigh me, so she would know exactly what she owed. Eight pounds, eleven ounces.

"And she never went," I said.

"She meant to. Every December she said next year. Then there was no money. Then there was no country. Then there was no time." She turned her cup. "She saved for the trip twice that I know of. The first time, in nineteen ninety-six, the money turned into two tickets to Miami instead."

I did the math at her table, on the back of a pharmacy receipt, because numbers are what I do when I can't feel anything yet. Tape, buckle, key, meter: two pounds, nine ounces. Forty-three years, and nothing collected while she lived, and no interest now. The debt was the weight of the baby, not the weight of the man.

"He's patient," Tía said, with approval. "He waited her whole life. He's not waiting anymore. He's collecting."

On the twenty-fifth of October I ran a test, because I am who I am. I put my best pliers in the steel box in the truck and padlocked it. In the morning the lock hung untouched and the pliers sat inside. I felt stupid until ten that night, when I reached for my wedding ring in the dish by the kitchen sink, and the dish was empty. The twenty-sixth still had two hours in it. He doesn't take what you offer. He takes what he chooses.

The part I had trouble saying out loud, even to Maritza, is this. I asked the jeweler at the Mall of the Americas what a band like mine weighs. A quarter of an ounce, maybe less. Eighteen years of marriage, credited at a quarter of an ounce. I checked the terms every way I knew how, and they always came out fair. Fair is by weight. Fair is not kind.

I took it to a priest at St. Brendan, my mother's parish. Father Echevarría, Cuban, about my age, fixes his own AC, he told me inside of five minutes. He listened without once looking at me like a crazy person. Then he told me honestly that the Church has no form for this. A vow is between the person and God. He could say a Mass for her. His own grandmother died owing the Virgin of Regla a yellow dress, he said. I asked what came of that. "Nothing," he said. "Or nothing we caught." He blessed me in the parking lot, and it fixed nothing, and I was glad I went.

Driving home down Eighty-Seventh Avenue I finally got angry. I never signed anything. I was eight months old. She promised my weight like I was a thing on a scale, and then everyone who agreed to the deal died and left the bill in my mailbox.

Nobody ever asked me.

Then, at the light at Bird Road, the other thought arrived, quiet. Eight pounds, eleven ounces of wax. That was the price, and look at everything it bought: Maritza, the truck, the house, forty-three years of mornings. If I refused to pay, I was the one saying my life wasn't worth eight pounds of candles. And if it counted the same paid angry as paid glad, I might as well pay it the way she always meant to. Walking in. Saying the names.

I flew in November. Miami to Bogotá, Bogotá to Caracas, two thousand one hundred dollars, then nine hours by road up into the green of Trujillo state, the business closed for eight days.

Isnotú is a small town on the side of a mountain, and it knows what it is. There's a shrine, and walls of little plaques saying thank you, and letters under glass, a lot of them about fevers. Outside, the woman who sold me the wax had a balance scale, the old kind. I told her eight pounds, eleven ounces, less two pounds, nine and a quarter, already collected. She didn't ask one question. She weighed it out like a pharmacist, exact, and wrapped it in newspaper. People bring her exact numbers every day of her life.

Six pounds of candles is heavier than it sounds, going uphill in that sun. I carried them in my arms, not in a bag, because the bag felt like cheating, and I don't know where I got that rule. Maybe the same place my mother got hers. At the shrine I lit three and left the rest in the racks with the others, where the wax of a thousand promesas runs together until you can't say whose is whose.

I said her name. María Eugenia. I said mine, Rafael, the boy from La Victoria, in case there was any confusion in the books. I said, está pagado.

Then I sat for a while on a low wall in the shade, not praying, just sitting. I thought about her at thirty-one, on her knees on clinic tile, promising the only currency she had. I thought about her in Miami, at our kitchen sink, the same saint watching from the hallway, December coming every year, the trip money saved twice and spent on me twice. The promesa money became the tickets, and the tickets became me, standing in Isnotú with empty arms and the smell of wax. I don't know how to call that a broken promise. I don't know how to call it kept, either.

On the twenty-sixth of December I checked the truck in the morning, slowly, tool by tool. Nothing was missing. On the twenty-sixth of January, nothing. The tape, the buckle, the key, the meter, the ring — none of it ever came back. Paid is paid. The new band Maritza picked out is lighter than the old one, and I feel the difference at the sink, every time.

Before I left Isnotú I bought two extra candles with my own money. No scale, no newspaper, just bought. This October, on his day, I lit one in the kitchen, and Maritza asked who it was for. A doctor my family used to know, I told her. The other one is in the drawer with the good batteries, where I can find it in the dark. It's not owed to anybody. Some things you want in the house before you need them.
