# Channel Nine

We bought the walkie-talkies at a garage sale on Mellen Lane the first Saturday of summer vacation, nineteen ninety-three. Two dollars for the pair. Danny talked the man down from three by pointing out that one antenna had teeth marks in it, and the man looked at Danny for a second and said fair enough, and that was the whole negotiation. At ten years old, that is as close as you get to closing a business deal, and Danny floated on it for a week.

Two dollars was real money. Two dollars was four lawns of edging at fifty cents a lawn, or a whole week of ignoring the ice cream truck, which took more discipline than either of us had. So we split it, a dollar each, half ownership, with one condition written into the deal: if we ever sold them, Danny kept the one without the teeth marks.

Jupiter Farms back then was dirt roads and drainage canals and slash pine, houses on acre lots far enough apart that you couldn't hear anybody's television but your own. The tap water smelled like sulfur. The rain came every afternoon around four, regular as a bus, and afterward the whole neighborhood steamed like something lifted out of an oven. We had bikes. We had a fort on the third canal. We had a brown dog named Gator who belonged to Danny but ate dinner at my house. And now we had radios.

The range was about half a mile, which was one canal more than the distance between our houses, and that fact alone was worth the two dollars. We tested every channel riding away from each other down the dirt, doing radio checks like we were landing airplanes. Channel nine came in cleanest. So channel nine was ours.

The voice showed up on the third night.

I was in bed with the radio under my pillow, because we had a system. Three clicks meant good night. Two clicks meant come to the window. I sent my three clicks, and instead of clicks I got a man's voice, quiet, like somebody trying not to wake the house.

"Danny? Am I on with Danny?"

"This is Pete," I said. Everybody called me Petey that summer. Only one person still does.

"Petey," the voice said, slow, like the name cost him something. "Right. Okay. Sorry. Wrong night." And he was gone, and I lay there with my heart going, and decided not to mention it to anyone, and told Danny first thing in the morning.

We spent the next day interrogating channel nine from the fort. Danny held the radio like a detective on television. "Whoever this is, this is a private channel. We have a receipt."

Nothing, for a long time. Then the voice came back, and you could hear the embarrassed in it. "I know it's your channel. I'm sorry. Can I say one thing first?"

"Say it to who?"

"To you, Danny."

And Danny, who didn't know yet that anything strange was happening, said, "Who are you?"

The voice took a breath the way you do at the doctor's. "This is going to sound like a crazy person talking. I'm you. I'm Danny, grown up, older than your dad is now. I don't understand the radio part any better than you're about to, and I think I only get the one summer. So I'd like to use it, if that's all right."

We did the only reasonable thing, which was to say no way, shut the radio off, ride straight to my house, and turn the radio back on within the hour.

We mostly didn't believe him. I want to be fair to us: we were ten, not stupid. We made him take tests. What am I holding up right now. I can't see you, he said, I'm not there, it isn't television. Fine, who wins the World Series. I was ten that year, he said. I wasn't keeping track of baseball. The Marlins are going to lose a lot, but they're brand new. Everybody loses their first year. It doesn't tell you anything about the team. Danny said that was the worst psychic answer he'd ever heard, and the voice agreed with him.

He knew things, but he knew them the way you remember things, which is to say badly. He knew about the fort but put it on the second canal, and when Danny corrected him he went quiet and then said you're right, the second one kept flooding, I'm sorry, it's been thirty years. He knew the shortcut behind the pump house. He knew our clicks. He asked once, fast, like he'd been holding it in all week, "How's Gator," and before either of us could answer he said never mind, don't tell me, just give him the fat end of the hot dog sometimes. Not always. Sometimes.

A man with a scanner could have known half of that, and we said so, to each other and to him. Some dad playing a long joke. A trucker with a big antenna and nothing else going on. He never argued. That was what didn't fit any of our theories: he agreed with our theories. That's probably the smart way to see it, he'd say. Believe that one. Keep talking to me anyway.

So we kept talking to him anyway. It was summer, he was interesting, and the bar is not high when you're ten.

Here is what I knew about Danny's house that summer, in the way kids know things, which is sideways. Danny's dad worked for a pool company until May, and then he didn't. His truck in the driveway before dark meant something different at Danny's house than my dad's truck meant at mine. Danny could read a driveway from the corner of the street the way other kids read a pitcher's windup, and depending on the read we'd ride past, one more lap around the canals, sometimes two, sometimes until the streetlights. He had a theory that summer, and he worked it hard: if he kept his bike out of the carport, and kept quiet at the right times, and didn't ask for things at the store, the house stayed level. When it didn't stay level, he'd go looking for what he'd done. He always found something, because if you go looking, you always find something.

He kept a duffel bag packed under his bed. He said it was hurricane supplies. It was June, and the bag had his baseball cards in it.

Some nights I'd send three clicks and get back one click, which wasn't in the system, and I knew it meant his volume was all the way down because of how the house was. On those nights the voice didn't talk either. I know because I'd lie there listening. Channel nine just hissed, and somehow even the hiss sounded like someone keeping a kid company.

The night I'm actually trying to tell you about was in July, after a loud one. Danny slept over. We had the radio on the bed between us and the fan going, and the voice asked if Danny was there, and Danny said yeah, and the voice said, "I need to say the thing now. You don't have to believe me. You just have to hear it."

I won't get the words exact. It's been thirty-three years. But what he said was that the thing wrong in that house was wrong before Danny was ever born. That it wasn't a test Danny was failing. That the bike out of the carport had never once made a difference, not because Danny did it wrong, but because it was never about the bike, and a kid can't fix a grown man, and he was allowed to stop trying. He said the score Danny kept on himself could stop now. And then he said the one part I do have exact, because he said it twice. You were a good kid. You're a good kid. Nobody said it to me that summer, so I'm saying it.

Danny lay there looking at the ceiling fan. He said, "Okay." Then he said, "I gotta use the bathroom," and was gone a while, and came back with his face washed, and we didn't talk about it, because we were ten and there was nothing to say that the fan wasn't already saying.

I can tell you what changed, though. Nobody explains anything when you're ten; you go by evidence. He still read the driveway. But in August he ate the last popsicle at my house without doing the thing where he asks twice if I'm sure. He stopped apologizing when Gator tracked mud on our porch, mud being, you understand, not a thing Danny did. Small stuff. Driveway-gravel small. It was the biggest summer of my life, and I knew it while it was happening, which almost never gets to be true.

We never told anybody. Kids understand this better than adults do: some things are only true on channel nine, and if you carry them off the channel, they break.

The calls thinned out in August. He never sounded scared about it, just farther off, like a station you're driving away from. Danny asked him once, near the end, "Is it good where you are?" The voice thought about it longer than a liar would have. "It took a while," he said. "It's good."

All summer he'd been saying goodbye wrong, like it was a telephone, and all summer Danny had corrected him: you say over, or you say out, and you don't say both unless you're done for good. The last night he called, he thanked us for the channel. He said he didn't know if it had helped us any, but it had helped him to say it, and he was sorry for taking up our summer. Danny told him he could have the channel on Tuesdays. The voice did a laugh that wasn't at anything. Then he said over and out, correctly, for the first time all summer, and channel nine went back to being hiss, and us.

School started. The rain let up. The batteries died in October and the walkies went into a drawer, the way the holy objects of a summer always go into a drawer.

I'm forty-three now. That's about what the voice sounded like, I think, though when I was ten, every man past thirty sounded the same age, which was old.

I don't know who it was. At this distance it would be easy to round the story off. Make it a scanner, make it a miracle, make it whichever one sleeps best. A man with a radio and a soft heart, who'd grown up in a loud house himself and recognized one from half a mile off. That's possible. I've had thirty-three years, and possible is as far as I ever get, in either direction, and I've stopped calling that a failure. What I know is plainer than that. Somebody told Danny the truth the summer nobody else was going to, on a two-dollar radio, and the telling held. And saying it cost him something. You could hear that part. That part I never doubted.

Danny got out of that house at seventeen, and it wasn't simple, and it isn't mine to tell. He installs air conditioners out of Port Saint Lucie now, and he's good at it, the kind of good where strangers ask for him by name. He has a daughter. She turned ten in April.

We talk most Sundays. Last Sunday I caught him in the middle of her birthday party, and he didn't hurry off the phone. I could hear the party going behind him. I heard him hold the phone away and tell her the cake could wait two minutes, he was talking to Petey, and I heard her laugh at the name. You can tell a lot about a man by how he talks to a ten-year-old. Somebody taught him. I won't tell you who, because I don't know.

Before we hung up, he said it the way we taught the voice. Or the way the voice taught us. I honestly can't remember anymore which direction it went.

Over, and out.
