# The Notary

My office is eleven feet wide, between Tropical Nails and a phone repair place, in a strip mall on Lake Worth Road that the bus drivers call the one with the flag store. The window says NOTARY, NOTARIO, FAX, FINGERPRINTS. The State of Florida lets me charge ten dollars per notarial act, which is the state's way of making sure nobody does this job for the money.

People think a notary witnesses signatures. That's the smallest part. Mostly I administer oaths. You stand in my office with the box fan going and a pedicure happening on the other side of the wall, you raise your right hand, and you swear that something is true. Then I stamp it. The stamp doesn't make it true. The stamp certifies that a human being was willing to swear.

That's how it's supposed to work.

In March a man named Wendell brought in an affidavit of residency for his daughter's school. The address was his cousin's apartment on Davis Road, where he could shower and collect mail but not, strictly, live. He had been sleeping in a Corolla behind the Tire Kingdom on Dixie for five weeks. He held the pen like it might go off.

A notary doesn't judge the contents, only the oath, and I have gotten very good at only the oath. He swore. I stamped. Ten dollars; I gave him five back because of how he held the pen.

Two days later he was in my doorway asking what I had done. The landlord had called him. A ground-floor unit had opened up, which it hadn't. Wendell's name was already on the lease, which it couldn't have been. The rent was the number Wendell could pay, exactly, like a shirt taken in by somebody who knew his size. He resided there now. The affidavit said so.

I want to tell you I was sensible about this. What I actually did was write out a sworn statement that the air conditioner in unit 4 was in good working order, and ask Esperanza from the nail salon to swear it. It had been dead since October. Esperanza read it, looked at me the way you look at a coworker who has started talking to God, raised her hand, and swore. I stamped. The vent coughed twice and started breathing cold. Esperanza crossed herself and went back to her two o'clock.

I learned the rules the way you learn any machine, by feeding it paper. It worked only on statements a person would raise a hand for, and only in the moment the seal bit the page, though what changed stayed changed. And it would not work for me. A notary cannot notarize her own signature; it's the first thing they teach you. In the third drawer of my desk is an affidavit I have wanted true for two years. It says my mother, Ligia, of Barquisimeto, is eighty-one, and well, and has her papers, and is coming. I tried once anyway, alone at night, hand raised in an empty office. The stamp left nothing but ink.

In April, Carmen Ojeda came in with a custody attestation for her grandson, Mateo, who is six. Her daughter was in Kissimmee, getting better, which is a sentence you don't ask follow-ups about. The form asked whether she was physically able to provide daily care, and Carmen's hands are what arthritis makes, knuckles like ginger root. Mateo had been doing his own buttons since five, and hers too. She stared at the box a long time. I asked her, do you care for him? She said, "Every day of his life." I said, then swear it that way. She swore. I stamped.

She came back Friday with a sleeve of Publix cookies and opened the cellophane in front of me, slowly, with her own fingers, like a card trick. We didn't discuss it.

After that I stopped pretending this was happening to me, and started doing it. I learned to ask the right question before the oath, so the true version of a person's life got onto the paper instead of the scared version. An affidavit of support for a nephew in Maracaibo. A residency form for a family in a converted garage that, by Tuesday, was a legal unit with a permit nobody remembered issuing. A man who wrote out, in his own hand, I am done drinking, and asked if a thing like that could be notarized, and swore it like a man grabbing a rail. I stamped it. That one I don't know about. I hope it held.

In May a woman came in from the county Records Integrity Unit, wearing a lanyard and a cardigan in ninety-one degrees. Her name was Ms. Pell, and she was kind the whole time, which is the part I need you to understand. She complimented my office. She accepted a cup of water. Then she opened a folder and told me that in fourteen months, not one document I had notarized had been challenged, amended, contested, or revoked. Not one. The county average, she said, is four percent. She said, "Zero is not a good number. Zero is a flag."

I asked if I was being accused of something. She looked genuinely hurt. "This isn't an investigation," she said. "We don't use that word at this stage. We like to resolve things while they're still friendly." She asked me to bring my seal and journal to the office on Olive Avenue on Friday, to be certified. "Routine," she said, gathering her folder. "You certify things all day. You understand."

I understood.

Thursday I opened at seven. Word moves through a strip mall faster than through a family, and by eight the line went past the flag store. Residencies, attestations, a guardianship, a letter swearing a roof did not leak, sworn by the woman who slept under it. I charged nobody, which I know looks worse. Esperanza brought me lunch and stood by the fax machine like a bodyguard.

Friday morning I drove to Olive Avenue and handed my seal across a desk to Ms. Pell, who put it in a padded envelope, the kind you'd mail a phone in, and gave me a form titled Voluntary Relinquishment of Commission. Nobody said fraud. Nobody said anything; that was the trick of the whole building. She thanked me for my cooperation and offered me a mint, and I took the mint, because my mother raised me right and there was nothing else in that room to take.

Maybe she knew what the stamp was. Maybe the county has an envelope for these. She was too polite to say, and I was in no position to ask.

I'm still in the plaza. Esperanza's brother razored NOTARIO off the glass and left FAX and FINGERPRINTS. When people ask for the notary, I send them to the UPS Store on Jog Road, where the stamp is just a stamp.

Wendell's daughter made student of the month in April. He brought the certificate by. It had a gold seal, embossed, official, and he grinned and asked if I wanted to touch it. Joking. Not entirely.

Last Thursday I saw Carmen and Mateo in the cereal aisle of the Publix on Dixie. He pushed the cart and she steered him by the shoulder. At the register she counted exact change out of a snap purse, fast, with fingers that worked, and she did not notice she was doing it.

My mother's affidavit is still in the third drawer, unstamped. The county has my seal in an envelope on Olive Avenue, certified, filed, harmless. They believe they took it out of circulation, and they are right, and it doesn't matter.

Wendell resides. Carmen does buttons.

The paper said so. The paper holds.
