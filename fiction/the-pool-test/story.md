# The Pool Test

The system went in two summers ago at the Meadows Park pool in Boynton Beach, after what happened in 2019, which nobody at this pool talks about. Eight cameras, four of them under the waterline, software from a company in Haifa. The salesman said it had watched eleven million hours of human swimming, and that the hardest problem his industry ever solved was telling a person from everything else — a shadow, a towel, a beach ball. The city paid. The insurance company insisted. That's how safety usually arrives in Florida.

I'm the lifeguard. I've been guarding water since I was sixteen, and I'll say this on the record: the thing was flawless. In March it flagged a six-year-old eleven seconds before I'd have seen him, because drowning kids go quiet, and quiet is what it watches for. In May it caught an old man's stroke going ragged and called a cardiac event before the man felt it. You can't resent a machine that keeps handing you back children.

It had one bug. Evenings, the headcount ran one low. Six swimmers in the water, the screen said five. A technician flew in, recalibrated, ate lunch at the Flanigan's on Congress, flew home. The count stayed one low. Evenings only. We stopped filing tickets.

The seven-o'clock woman swam evenings. Navy suit, white cap, a freestyle so smooth it didn't leave a wake, fifty laps and never once out of breath. I never saw her arrive; she was just in the water when the shadows got long. She told me her name was Alice. Once, when a kid was crying through a float lesson, she stood up in the shallow end and said, "The water holds you if you stop arguing with it," and the kid floated, and Alice went back to her laps.

Last Tuesday the pool was empty at dusk except for her. At 7:14 she went down by the lane rope in the deep end and did not come up.

At ninety seconds I was standing. The board was green. The system draws a yellow box around every swimmer it sees, so I pulled the underwater feed, and there she was on the bottom, cross-legged, eyes open, still as a photograph. No box.

I don't have a good account of the next eighteen minutes. My hand was on the manual alarm the whole time. The feed showed a woman who was not drowning — not struggling, not sinking, just there, the way the drain is there. Every minute I decided to go in, and every minute the screen said, in its green way, that there was no one in this pool. At 7:34 I stopped asking its permission and went in.

She came up easy. She weighed nothing in the water, and at the ladder she weighed what a person weighs. She was breathing. I held her wrist and found a pulse. I checked twice. I found one twice. Slow, like something remembering to do it. She patted my arm the way you comfort a rescuer. "You didn't have to do that, baby. The deep end is the quietest place in Boynton Beach." Then she did two more laps while I sat dripping on the deck, and climbed out, and said good night, and went toward the gate. I made myself watch the water instead. By then I think I knew.

In the office I pulled the log. The system files everything it sees as SWIMMER, STAFF, or DEBRIS. From 7:14 to 7:34, the deep end of the Meadows Park pool contains nothing. Water. At 7:34 the record shows one staff member entering the deep end, diving, and surfacing. Alone. Eleven million hours of telling people from everything else, and it looked straight at her, and it made its call.

There's a bench by the gate I'd walked past a hundred times. Bronze plaque, small letters. IN MEMORY OF ALICE COSTA, 1951–2019. SHE LOVED THIS WATER. The system was installed the following spring. It exists because of her. It has never made a mistake.

She was back Thursday at seven. Navy suit, white cap, fifty laps. The count still runs one low, and we let it. But I keep the paper log now, the one the state stopped requiring when the cameras came, and every evening I count the pool myself and write down a number, and my number is one higher than the machine's.

I stand by my count.
