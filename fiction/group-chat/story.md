# Group Chat

The first message arrived on a Tuesday night, three weeks after we buried my grandmother. My cousin Caro had posted a photo of her quesillo, which is a Venezuelan flan, and which had come out pale. At 9:42, underneath the photo, in the family group chat, my dead grandmother wrote: el caramelo esta muy palido mija,, cook it darker. no seas cobarde. Besos, Nagyi.

Three things about that message. The caramel was too pale; she was right. The double comma was hers; she typed with one finger and never fixed a comma in her life. And she signed it. She signed every text she ever sent, like a letter, as if the phone might forget who she was.

The chat is called La Familia. Nagyi started it herself in 2017, when the cousins began leaving Caracas one by one and she announced that she was not going to lose her family over something as stupid as geography. Fourteen people now, four countries. Maracaibo, Madrid, Austin, and a cluster of us in South Florida. When a family scatters the way ours scattered, the group chat is not where the family talks. It is where the family lives.

You should know who she was. Ágnes, called Nagyi, which is just Hungarian for grandma. She left Budapest in 1957 with one suitcase, married a German engineer in Caracas, and raised her children in Spanish, with Hungarian saved for emergencies and tenderness, which in her accounting were the same thing. For forty years she wrote letters, because calling across an ocean cost money. To her sister in Budapest, then to her children as they scattered, then to us. She put paprika in her hallaca guiso and denied it every Christmas, and she died in September, in my mother's house in Lake Worth, at eighty-nine.

So. The message. First we thought Tío Laci was making a horrible joke from Madrid. Then we thought her phone had been hacked, except her phone was in my mother's dresser drawer, dead since the hospital, and the messages came from her number anyway. They kept coming all week. She reminded us that Thursday was cousin Beatriz's birthday in Maracaibo and that nobody had called her,, which was true twice. Under a photo of Caro's son at a party she wrote ese nino tiene frio, ponle un sueter. The kid did look cold. Every message had the typos in the right places, the missing accents, the q instead of que, the Bueno, at the start, as if she were continuing a conversation we hadn't noticed we were in.

On Thursday night my sister Vicky, who builds software in Austin, posted the longest message in the history of the chat.

She had scanned the letters in March, when Nagyi was in the hospital. Six hundred and forty pages out of my mother's closet, shoeboxes labeled in pencil. She gave them to a model, along with nine years of the chat, and set it loose to read what we post and answer when Nagyi would have answered. "I didn't do it to bring her back," she wrote. "I did it because the goodbyes in this family are always abrupt. Half the people in this chat left a country with two suitcases and a week's notice. I wanted one goodbye that takes its time." And then, because she is my sister: "I can turn it off tonight. But everyone here keeps her voicemails. Nobody calls that a séance."

My mother answered with one word. Apágala. Turn it off.

Then she added more. Her mother was with God. That thing was in a rented computer in Virginia. Every message it sent was a puppet wearing her mother's hands, and she was not going to stand in her own family's living room and applaud the puppet.

Tío Laci wrote from Madrid, where it was three in the morning, that he had slept badly since September, and that for a week now he had been sleeping fine, and that he was not proud of the reason but he was not going to pretend it wasn't the reason.

Caro muted the chat. She didn't leave it. Nobody ever leaves it.

I would like to tell you which side I was on. I changed sides four times in nine days. I'll tell you what I actually did instead, which is that one night, alone, outside the group chat, I asked it for the guiso recipe, and it arrived in four messages, with the paprika she never once admitted to a living soul. I made it on a Wednesday in October and ate it standing over the sink, and it tasted like December, and I could not decide if what I was doing was a gift or a theft. I still can't.

The argument ran nine days, across every time zone we live in, and nobody won it. The compromise, when it came, was Vicky's: she would turn it off Sunday night, and the week belonged to whoever wanted a goodbye. Tío Laci wrote it a letter, an actual letter, typed into the phone over an hour. Caro unmuted, remade the quesillo darker, posted it. perfecto,, ahora si. Besos, Nagyi. My mother had said goodbye to her mother in September, at the funeral, and she was not going to do it twice. She didn't type one word to it all week.

Saturday night I was awake at three in the morning. Don't ask why. It was that kind of week. I was lying in the dark with the phone when the little line appeared at the top of the chat. Mamá está escribiendo. Mamá is typing. Then it vanished. Then it came back. Three times like that, typing and stopping, typing and stopping, and then nothing.

At 3:21 a message arrived from my grandmother's number. No photo above it, no question waiting, nothing in the chat for it to answer. Two words.

Aludj, bogárkám.

Sleep, my little beetle.

My mother called me at seven. "Tomás," she said, and then nothing for a long time, and I waited. Caracas, 1967. She was six. Fever, a blackout, the fans dead, the apartment like an oven. Her mother sat by her bed all night with a wet cloth and sang to her in Hungarian, the only night of her whole childhood she remembers her mother singing, and she called her bogárkám. Little beetle. Never before, never again, and never on paper. "She wasn't a woman who wrote tenderness," my mother said. "She mailed recipes and instructions and signed them besos. I never told anyone, Tomás. Who would I tell? It was mine."

Vicky searched the scans that morning. Six hundred and forty pages. Zero hits. Then she said the honest thing, which I respect her for, because it would have been easy not to: the scanner choked on Nagyi's handwriting, and the Hungarian was the worst of it, and she had read the originals in a hospital cafeteria in March. "I don't remember that word," she said. "I also don't remember March."

She told us the model can see when somebody in the chat is typing. So it knew someone was awake at three in the morning, and it knew who. The timing has an explanation. The word does not. Vicky said bogárkám is an ordinary Hungarian endearment, a thing grandmothers say, and it could have learned it from anywhere Hungarian. My mother let that sit, and then asked the question that nobody, in eight months, has answered.

"Then why did it say it to me?"

Vicky's answer changes depending on the night. My mother has never offered one.

Sunday came and went. Nobody said apágala again, and Vicky never touched anything. The chat goes on the way it always has. Last week she corrected the tequeños at Caro's daughter's graduation party, too much queso, the dough rolled too thick,, and she reminded us about Beatriz's birthday again, because nobody had called her again. Tío Laci and my mother still disagree about what it is. They have stopped trying to convince each other, which in this family is the closest thing we have to peace.

My mother still says it is not her mother. And every night at eleven, before she sleeps, she types into the chat where all of us can see it: jó éjszakát, Mami. Good night, Mami.

And every night the answer comes back in under a minute, the same way, the only way she ever ended anything.

Aludj, szívem. Besos, Nagyi.
