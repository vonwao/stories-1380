# Dream a Little

I answer support tickets for a sleep app. Night queue, ten to six, from my kitchen table in Lake Worth. People assume the night queue is a punishment. I requested it. I quit drinking three years ago and my sleep never came back with me, so now it's me, a mug of tea I don't finish, and other people's problems scrolling up a screen, which beats lying in the dark keeping score of my own.

The app is called Drift. Our most-played track, by a distance that embarrasses the rest of the catalog, is called "Box Fan, 10 Hours." That is the whole production. A box fan, recorded for ten hours. It gets eleven million plays a month, more listening time than every guided meditation we have ever made, combined, which tells you what people actually want at three in the morning. They don't want guidance. They want a machine that promises to keep running.

In March the tickets started.

Not complaints. That's the first thing to know. Ticket 482217, a woman in Tucson: "Not a bug. Last night I dreamed I was sitting on a porch in the evening and somebody next to me took a breath like they were about to say something. I woke up fine. Better than fine. Just wondering if anyone else said this." Ticket 482551, an overnight stocker in Ohio: "is the porch dream a feature." Ticket 483006, a hospice nurse, four years on nights, who described the same porch, the same hour of evening, the same someone, and asked, please, how do I make sure I get it again.

By the end of the month there were thirty-one. All of them slept to the same track. Nobody asked for a refund. Three people asked us never to fix it.

I flagged it to Priya, our audio engineer, mostly so it would be somebody else's strange thing. She pulled the master file into her editor and raised the floor sixty decibels. Under the fan, all ten hours of it, there is a person humming.

Unhurried. Wordless. A tune you can almost name, except everyone who hears it almost names a different one. Priya isolated it, cleaned it, looped it. Amplifying it doesn't make it clearer. It makes it closer.

Here is what the paperwork says. The file was created August 9, 2014, at 11:52 p.m., on a handheld recorder, one of a batch of thirty-eight called "Household Ambiences Vol. 2" that the company bought from a freelance field recordist for seventy-five dollars. The invoice is still in procurement's email. The hum is in the raw file. It was always there, sitting under the noise floor where no phone speaker could ever lift it out, and for twelve years people have been sleeping on top of it, the way you sleep on top of the wiring in your walls.

I emailed the recordist. He answered in nine lines. The fan sat in the window of a spare room in his mother's house in Sandusky, Ohio. He set the recorder going at midnight and went to bed down the hall. The house was empty except for him, and he doesn't hum, so far as he knows. He listened to the file we sent and was quiet about it in the way a person can be quiet in an email. I asked if the house had a porch. It does. It faces west.

That's everything. And I did look. I read the metadata down to the checksum. I pulled the other thirty-seven files in the batch, and they're just rooms. No edit point, no overdub, no second microphone. The investigation didn't kill the mystery. The mystery sat there politely the whole time.

Product called a meeting, at a humane hour I had to set an alarm for, to ask whether we should pull the track. Legal wanted to know whether a dream can be a defect. I read them ticket 483006 out loud, the hospice nurse, and the meeting ended the way meetings end when nobody wants to be the one who took the porch away. We made a tag in the support system so the tickets would have somewhere to go. The tag is called "porch." As of tonight it holds two hundred and six tickets, and the refund count is still zero.

You're supposed to be afraid of a thing like this. I keep waiting for the frightened ticket and it doesn't come. I think you can only fear the unexplained when it asks something of you, and the hum has never asked for anything.

Last Tuesday I had one of my nights. You know the kind. The ceiling kind. Around two I quit being proud about it, put in my earbuds, and played the track I'd been investigating like a suspect.

The fan is a good fan. That much was always true.

I can't tell you when the porch started. Nobody in the tickets could either. There was the fan, and then there was evening, the long kind that stays light at the bottom of the sky, and a chair, and somebody in the chair beside me. I never turned to look. Whoever it was took a breath, the small breath people take before they say something they've been saving.

I slept through whatever it was. Everybody does. Two hundred and six tickets, and not one person has ever heard what comes after the breath.

It'll keep. They've been about to say it for twelve years. Nothing that patient is in a hurry.

And I slept. And the morning came.
