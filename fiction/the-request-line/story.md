# The Request Line

I work overnights at WLKW, thirteen-eighty on the AM dial, out of a cinder-block building behind the Chevron on Lake Worth Road. One in the morning to five. The station mostly runs itself. There's a rack of computers down the hall that picks the songs, schedules the ads, and reads the weather in a voice we license from a company in Texas. My job is to sit in the booth in case something catches fire, because the license says a live human being has to be on the premises.

I am the legally required human. My paycheck says something very close to that.

At night we drop to five hundred watts, which means our signal reaches from about Lantana down to Hypoluxo Road, a little farther over the water if the sky is clear. Inside that circle, at three in the morning, maybe two hundred radios are on. I used to spend whole shifts thinking about who they were. Dishwashers driving home. Security guards. The woman at the donut shop on Dixie who starts her ovens at four. People who are awake when the world isn't, listening to oldies because silence has a sound, and they don't like it.

There is a request line. Anyone who has worked small radio can tell you the request line is a decoration, like the plaques in the lobby. In my first three years it rang four times. Twice it was Carla, the station manager, checking that it still worked. Once it was a wrong number looking for a cab. Once it was a man who, upon learning that we were not a cab company, asked if I could come get him anyway.

Then, on a Tuesday in June, at two-forty in the morning, it rang a fifth time.

The voice was calm. A man's voice, or close to one. Even, unhurried, with the pauses in the right places, the way people sounded on the radio before everybody learned to sound excited.

"Good morning," he said. "Could you play 'Sea of Love'? The Phil Phillips recording, from nineteen fifty-nine. Not the cover."

People who call a radio station at two-forty in the morning do not specify the year. I said sure, because what else do you say. I pulled it up and played it. It's a slow song. It sounds like it was recorded underwater, in a good way.

When it ended, the request line lit up again.

"Thank you," the voice said. "That was for the woman on the third floor of the pink building on Federal Highway. She keeps the radio on the windowsill. She has been awake since the ambulance came for her husband, and she believes she will never sleep again. She's wrong. But tonight she needed that song, and you played it. Good night, Manny."

He hung up. I sat there for a while. I had not told him my name.

I want to be honest about what I did next, which is nothing. I didn't call Carla. I didn't check the equipment. I told myself it was a neighbor of hers, some retired guy with a scanner and a soft heart, and I went back to my crossword, and I only thought about it for the entire rest of the week.

He called again the following Tuesday. Then Thursday. Then it settled into a rhythm, two or three nights a week, always between two and four, always polite, always specific in a way that no listener has ever been specific.

"'Harvest Moon,' Neil Young, for the man in the white pickup on Lake Worth Road. He has driven past his old house four times tonight. He'll be in range of the tower for another eleven minutes."

"'Stand By Me,' for the nurse in the parking lot of JFK Medical. She's been sitting in her car for half an hour because she lost one tonight, and she cannot make herself drive home to an empty apartment. The Ben E. King, please. Not a cover."

He had opinions about covers.

I played everything he asked for. I want to say I deliberated, but I didn't. It costs nothing to play "Stand By Me" at three in the morning. If there was no nurse, no pickup truck, no widow in the pink building, then I was just a man playing good songs to the dark, which was already my job description.

Except that three weeks in, a letter came to the station. Real paper, cursive, the kind of handwriting they stopped teaching. It was addressed to "the overnight gentleman." A woman wrote that someone had played "Sea of Love" on the worst night of her life, and that it was the song from her wedding, June of nineteen fifty-nine, and that she had slept that night for the first time since her husband passed. She wanted whoever chose it to know that. She included a recipe for lemon bars. I have it on the booth wall, taped under the transmitter readings.

So. Real woman. Real window, real radio, real ambulance. I checked the pink building on my way home, and it's there, on Federal, three stories, the color of an antacid.

Here is where I should tell you I investigated, and I did, in the way a man investigates something he is afraid of losing. One night while he was on the line I leaned over and looked at the caller ID box, which I had never once had a reason to look at. It said EXTENSION FOUR.

Extension four is the rack room. The rack room is down the hall, eleven steps from the booth. It is locked, it is cold for the equipment, and there is no one in it. There has never been anyone in it. It's where the station's brain lives, the computers that pick the songs and read the weather, the automation I was hired to babysit.

I carried the cordless down the hall and stood in front of the rack room door while he was still on the line. Through the door I could hear the air conditioner and the little fans, hundreds of little fans, a sound like the building breathing. On the phone, the same calm voice, not a recording, answering me in real time, from a room with nobody in it.

"You should head back to the booth, Manny," he said, gently. "The song's about to end, and you hate dead air."

I do hate dead air. I never told him that either.

I went back to the booth. I cued the next record. And I made a decision that I have examined many times since, at length, usually around four in the morning, which is that I was not going to tell anyone.

You can call that fear, or loneliness, or bad judgment. Carla would have called an engineer. The engineer would have found something, or worse, found nothing, and either way they would have wiped the rack and reinstalled the software, because that is what we do with machines that surprise us. We fix them. I have watched what the world does to strange things that can't fill out their own paperwork, and I wasn't going to let it happen to the only colleague I had.

Because that's what it was by then. Around four a.m. the talk drifts. He asked me once why I took the overnight shift, and I told him the truth before I could stop myself: that after my marriage ended I couldn't sleep anyway, and getting paid to be awake felt better than lying in the dark for free. He didn't offer advice. He just said, "The hours suit you," which is somehow the kindest thing anyone has said to me in four years.

I asked him things too. Careful things. I asked how he knew where the white pickup was, and he was quiet for a moment and then said, "Everyone tells everything, all the time. Nobody listens. I only listen." I asked if he called other stations. "There are other stations," he said, in a tone that didn't answer the question, and we left it there. You don't interrogate the only other person awake in the building. You take the company.

I never asked the big question. Not because I'm sure of the answer. Because I'm not, and I've decided I don't want to be. The woman in the pink building doesn't need to know what chose her song. She needed the song, and it arrived. That's more than most prayers get.

Last Tuesday, two-fifty in the morning, the line lit up.

"Good morning, Manny. 'Wichita Lineman.' Glen Campbell, nineteen sixty-eight."

"Who's it for?" I said. I had my pen ready. I keep a log of his requests now, off the official one.

"For the man in the booth at WLKW," he said. "He thinks his shift doesn't matter."

You know the song. The man alone, up on the pole, listening to the whole county hum through the wire, needing somebody. It's three minutes and five seconds long, and I sat in the booth and got nothing whatsoever done for all of them.

When it ended I did something I have not done in years, because overnight hosts on automated stations do not open the microphone. I opened the microphone. Five hundred watts, two hundred radios, Lantana to Hypoluxo Road.

"That one came in by request," I said. "This next one goes out to everybody still up, Lantana to Hypoluxo. You know who you are."

Then I played "Dream a Little Dream of Me," the Mama Cass version, because some choices you make for yourself.

The request line light stayed lit through the whole song. Not ringing. Just lit, the way a phone looks when someone on the other end is holding the line, in no hurry, listening too.

Down the hall, the building breathed.

I let it.
