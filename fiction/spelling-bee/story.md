# Round Seven

I placed ninth in the Palm Beach County spelling bee in nineteen ninety-one. People are surprised I remember the year. Then I tell them I also remember the word, boutonniere, and that I left out the second n. I have not needed to spell boutonniere once in thirty-five years. I have also never stopped spelling it. My daughter says that is the saddest fact she knows about me, and she says it kindly, the way you would tell a man his shirt is inside out.

Mia is eleven. This spring she won the bee at her school in Wellington on the word alfalfa, which she spelled in about two seconds, the way you would say your own address. I clapped louder than any parent in that cafeteria, and I want to be honest about the clapping. Some of it was for her. A good portion was for a boy from nineteen ninety-one who was finally going back to county with better material.

So we trained. I printed the county study list, four thousand one hundred words, and cut it into flashcards at the kitchen table. We drilled in the car, on Forest Hill, on Southern, in the pickup line. Latin roots, Greek roots, the French words that hide their letters. Her mother said the bee had become my midlife crisis with flashcards, which was unkind, and accurate.

The Saturday practice club meets at a library in Royal Palm Beach. The coach, Mrs. Pell, a retired teacher, prints a fresh quiz list every morning and keeps it in a sealed envelope, because spelling parents are not above anything. Three Saturdays in, at a red light, I looked over and Mia was writing on the inside of her left wrist with a ballpoint. Small, neat words, climbing toward her elbow. I asked what she was doing. Getting ready, she said. Two hours later Mrs. Pell tore open her envelope and read out twelve words nobody on earth had seen, and they were Mia's twelve words, in Mia's order, and my daughter spelled every one of them with the expression of a kid unloading a dishwasher.

I checked the wrist in the parking lot. Twelve for twelve, written before nine in the morning, for a quiz that did not exist until eleven.

I would like to tell you I handled the next two weeks well. Where do they come from, I asked, and she shrugged the way eleven-year-olds shrug, with her whole skeleton. When do they show up? Breakfast, mostly. What does it feel like? She chewed on that for a block and a half. Like remembering, except forward. Then I behaved badly, and I want to describe it, because I do not think I am the only parent who would have. I asked her to write tomorrow's words tonight. They're not there yet, she said. I opened the dictionary at random and asked her to hear the word under my finger. I only get mine, Dad. One night, in a light voice that fooled neither of us, I suggested we film her doing it, just once, just for us. She gave me the look she gives the cafeteria pizza, which she maintains is wet bread with opinions, and she said, I'm not a trick.

She wasn't, either. That is the part I keep needing to say out loud. The whole time, she stayed exactly eleven. She had a sleepover at Dani's and came home with a friendship bracelet and a theory about a girl named Brianna. The words came to her the way mail comes to a house. Addressed, regular, nothing anybody in the house has to deserve.

Two weeks before county, on Southern Boulevard, she said it plainly. I'm going to lose at county.

I asked her why on earth.

She had her reasons lined up, and they were eleven-year-old reasons right up until they weren't. The winner goes to nationals in Washington at the end of May, on television, and she does not want to be on television. Dani's birthday is that same week. And then she looked out the window and gave me the last one. It wouldn't be me winning. It would be whatever tells me. I want to win things I win.

I argued. I gave a speech about gifts, about how you do not hand back something this rare, and somewhere in the middle of it I heard myself, a man telling a child she owed the world a performance of a thing nobody could explain. What I wanted was the stage in Washington, and a girl at a microphone, and me in the third row being the father of her. I wanted boutonniere to finally stop. I knew it while I was talking, and I kept talking anyway. That is the embarrassing part, so I am saying it.

She let me finish. She let me keep coaching, too, all the way to the end, which I understood later was its own kind of gift. She never promised me anything. Kids are honest like that, if you are listening. I wasn't, quite.

County was a Tuesday in March, in an auditorium off Okeechobee Boulevard in West Palm. Eighty-eight spellers, numbered placards, parents holding programs rolled into telescopes. That morning Mia ate waffles at seven-forty with the ballpoint next to her plate, and wrote her words up her wrist, and I was so busy being nervous that I did not count them. I should have counted them.

She was placard fifty-one. Round one she got lagoon. Round two, cardigan. The rounds move slowly, and the room makes a sound every time the bell rings, a soft oh, eighty-eight families absorbing one family's bad afternoon. Round four, menagerie. Round five she got obbligato, asked for the language of origin, Italian, which she did not need, and spelled o. b. b. l. i. g. a. t. o. into the microphone without hurrying. The asking, I understood later, was a costume. Normal kids ask. She was being a normal kid as carefully as she had ever been anything.

By round six there were fourteen left, and three of them were crying a little in advance. I do not remember her round six word. I was too busy believing, by then, that the year could end in Washington.

Round seven, the pronouncer gave her sarsaparilla. A word with two r's, two l's, and a first r that nobody says out loud, which is the entire trap of it. She asked for the definition. She asked for it in a sentence. She asked for the language of origin, Spanish, and the room went quiet, and my daughter leaned into the microphone and spelled, s. a. s. p. a. r. i. l. l. a.

The bell was very small, and very final.

She said thank you to the judges, and meant it, and walked off the stage like someone who has caught her bus. What I knew, and nobody else in that auditorium knew, was that sarsaparilla had been on her left wrist since seven-forty that morning. Correct. Both r's. Waiting under her sleeve while she misspelled it out loud, missing it exactly the way an honest kid who had only ever heard it would. Only one of us was going to get what we wanted that day. There was never a version where we both did.

She tied for thirteenth out of eighty-eight. Thirteenth is a normal amount of good. Nobody interviews thirteenth. She had set herself down exactly where no one would ever look twice, and it had cost her one letter.

In the lobby there were butter cookies for the eliminated. She took two, and texted Dani.

At the light on Okeechobee I looked over at her arm. Seven words, in her small print, starting at the wrist bone. I counted them at the light, and again in the parking lot of the taco place on Forest Hill, the one that puts pineapple on things. Seven. Not eight. Not twelve, like a practice Saturday. She had written exactly what she was going to face and not one word more. So at seven-forty that morning, over waffles, she had already known the whole shape of her day. Or chosen it. I do not know which. I have thought about the difference between those two things more than anything in my life.

I had the question in my mouth all through lunch. What are you. How does it arrive. What else do you hear that you are not saying. She was busy defending pineapple as a taco ingredient, a position I consider indefensible, and she was winning, because she was eleven and happy and out of the bee, and her best friend's birthday was in May, in Wellington, where she would be.

I did not ask. That is what I did with my want, in the end. I folded it up small and I put it away and I ate my tacos. If that does not sound like much, try it sometime, with the one question you would give anything to ask sitting across from you, dipping a chip.

I never found out what she is. What she is, is eleven. She throws back what she could keep. She holds Dani's seat at lunch. She spells alfalfa like her own address. The words that arrive early, addressed to her like mail — those are hers. They came to her, not to me. I was just driving the car.

The want is not all the way gone. I should be honest about that too. Some mornings, when she reaches across the table for the cereal, I look at her wrist. I am not proud of it, and I do it anyway. There is never anything written there. Just the friendship bracelet, blue and yellow, double-knotted, that Dani made her at the sleepover. I look. I pour the cereal.

We talk about lunch.
