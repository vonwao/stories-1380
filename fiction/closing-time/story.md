# Closing Time

I run the karaoke at the Pelican, a dive on Dixie Highway in Lake Worth, Thursday nights, nine to close. You put your name and your song into a tablet chained to the bar, and an app called SingQue, which costs the owner eleven ninety-nine a month, builds the rotation. My job is the microphone, the levels, and saying names right.

People think the songs are the show. The songs are not the show. The rotation is the show. A good rotation is the fairest line most people stand in all week. Nobody skips, nobody tips their way up, the dentist waits behind the dishwasher. I have run that line for nineteen years, and I am protective of it the way some men are protective of lawns.

In March, the line started editing itself.

Deb has sung "Me and Bobby McGee" every Thursday for six years. I could run her levels asleep. But the screen called her name and put up "Landslide" instead, and I reached for the mic to apologize, because a regular's song is a contract. Deb stood there reading the screen for a while. Then she said, "Leave it."

She sang it plain, no big finish, looking at the back wall. When it ended she walked straight out to the patio and called somebody, and she was out there long enough that her beer went warm, and nobody touched it.

There's a part I don't bring up at parties, which is that the log says she requested it. Time-stamped, from the tablet: Landslide, Deb, nine forty-one. The machine doesn't just change the song. It changes the paperwork.

It kept happening, two or three times a night. A kid who only ever sang Smash Mouth as a joke got "Vienna," by Billy Joel, and the joke did not survive the second verse. He sang the whole thing. Nobody laughed. Hector, who does Vicente Fernández when his ex-wife isn't in the room, got handed a song I'm not going to put in this story, and he read the screen for a long time, paid his tab, and went home. The machine never offered it to him again. It doesn't push. It asks once.

I called the company. The man on the phone told me the app cannot assign songs, that there is no feature by which the app could assign songs, and that he was marking my ticket resolved. Ticket forty thousand two hundred twenty-one. Resolved.

I'll tell you what actually bothered me, because it wasn't the impossible part. Picking the right song for the right stranger is my job. Nineteen years of watching some nervous first-timer grip the binder, and steering them off "Purple Rain" and onto something with a floor in it. The machine was doing my job, and doing it better, and it had never once seen anybody's face.

I got over it. Mostly. SingQue has been trying to update itself since April. Version six point one, bug fixes and performance improvements. Every night it asks. Every night I press Later.

Then it reached past the line.

Walt has been sitting at the end of that bar since before I had the job, and in all those years he has never once put his name in. Some people come to karaoke to be left alone in a room where there's singing, and that's allowed. That's sacred, actually. The tablet is by the register, ten feet from his stool, and I'd swear in court he's never touched it.

In May, the screen called him. Walt. "You Are My Sunshine."

Now, the machine can put a name on a screen. It can't say the name out loud. That part is still mine. I stood there with the mic half raised, and the room waited, and I made the call I'd been making all spring, which was to trust the thing.

"Walt," I said. "You're up."

He looked at the screen for about ten seconds. Then he put a twenty under his glass and walked out, and the door did its slow hiss, and somebody else sang.

He didn't come back the next Thursday, or the one after. Deb asked about him. I didn't have anything. I drove home those nights wanting to throw the tablet in the Intracoastal. Deb had needed her song. The kid had needed his. But maybe what Walt needed was to be left alone, and the machine had spent his privacy like it was the bar's money. I still don't know what it knows. I know it can't see the parking lot.

The last Thursday in June, he was back on his stool like weather. Two beers, no eye contact. I left him be. At closing, while I was coiling cables and the bar was down to the stools-on-tables hour, Walt walked over to the booth. He didn't look at the tablet. He looked at me.

"That song still in there?"

Every song is still in there. That's the thing about the catalog.

I typed it in myself, under my own name, and the machine left it alone. Walt took the mic like it might be hot. Four people left in the bar, all of them tired, none of them strangers. The intro to that song is about four seconds long.

He sang the first line by himself. Nobody let him sing the second one by himself.
