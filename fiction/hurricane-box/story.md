# The Hurricane Box

My mother kept a plastic bin on the top shelf of her bedroom closet, and every year on the first of June she took it down and packed it the same way. The deed to the house. The insurance policies in a freezer bag. Her passport, and my grandmother's rings taped inside a travel soap dish so they wouldn't rattle. An envelope of photo negatives nobody on earth could develop anymore. And her answering machine, wrapped in a bread bag from the Publix bakery, the cord coiled and rubber-banded along the side, like she might need to plug it in during a storm.

She called it the hurricane box. The rule was simple. If the water comes, you take the box and you go, and everything else is furniture.

The machine hadn't been connected to a phone since two thousand nine, when she gave up the landline. It was a gray GE from the mid nineties with a microcassette inside, thirty minutes a side, and the tape was full. She had been saving messages on it since before our father left, and she never once let anyone hear them. If you walked in while she was playing it, she pressed stop with her thumb without looking up, the way you close a door on a room you don't owe anybody. On certain afternoons you could hear it through her bedroom door. A murmur. Never words, just the shape of a voice, then the click, then my mother running water in the bathroom for longer than anyone needs to run water.

She died in March, in her chair, with the television on. She was eighty-one. I am forty-six, my sister Dee is fifty-one, and before the funeral we had not been in a room together in six years.

The will was four pages. Her lawyer has an office up a staircase on Atlantic Avenue, above a frozen yogurt place, and he read it to us at two thirty on a Thursday afternoon. The house on Northeast Ninth Street, three blocks off Swinton, bought in nineteen seventy-one for twenty-two thousand five hundred dollars, goes to both daughters equally. And then the sentence she paid him to write. Every decision regarding the house and its contents must be unanimous.

Dee laughed once, like a cough. I didn't. Our mother did not believe in therapists or referees. She believed in logistics. She knew we wouldn't talk, so she built a situation where we'd have to.

For two months we conducted the war through the house. The sale itself took one phone call; money was never what we fought about. We hired a realtor named Marisol from one of the offices on East Atlantic and we negotiated through her like she was a customs window. Dee approved the listing price from Charlotte and rejected my photographs of the kitchen. I scheduled the roof inspection and Dee moved it without asking. We exchanged seventeen text messages about a water bill for forty-one dollars and twelve cents, and not one of those messages was about water.

The furniture we sorted in a weekend. The box we could not sort, because the box held the machine, and on the machine we were not unanimous.

Dee's position: there is a place in Boca that converts old tapes for forty dollars, and we should hear what our mother kept. Her argument was better than I wanted it to be. She packed it with the deed, Dee said. You don't rank something with the deed unless you mean for it to survive you.

My position: it goes in the niche with her. The interment was already scheduled at the memorial gardens west of the interstate, her ashes in a wall, and the niche is ten inches by ten inches, and the machine is nine and a half inches wide. I measured it the week after the funeral, with her sewing tape, alone in her bedroom. I want to be honest about what that says about me. I had my answer ready before anyone asked the question.

Here is how Dee and I got to six years of silence, told the only way I know how to tell it. In two thousand sixteen Dee flew down after the fender bender at the Publix on Military Trail and went home with our mother's car keys in her purse. I had a new key cut at the hardware store on Federal for eleven dollars and gave it to Mom in a birthday card. The next year it was the checkbook. The year after that, the knobs off the stove, which Dee took off with a screwdriver and I put back on with the same screwdriver, because Mom kept it in the drawer by the sink and we are, whatever else we are, our mother's daughters. Every time Dee took something for her own good, I drove over and gave it back. Dee kept score. So did I.

The Christmas of the stove, Dee mailed her own house key back to our mother in an envelope with no note. Mom put it in the junk drawer with the batteries, and it was still there in March, and that is everything anyone needs to know about the three of us, if they know how to read a junk drawer.

People say what they think you owe the dead. Closure, they say, like a debt that runs in both directions. I don't believe it does. I think you don't get to learn somebody after they die. Whatever they didn't hand you while they were alive, you don't get to collect afterward just because they can no longer press stop. Dee disagrees. Dee thinks a mother is a country you have a right of return to. We loved the same woman with positions that cannot both be true, and the woman is gone, and the positions are what's left.

And now the part I never told Dee. In two thousand eighteen, while Mom was at Bethesda for her hip and a storm was circling the maps, I took the box down to have it ready by the door, and the bread bag had come loose, and the machine sat there with its little window showing the tape. I pressed play. A man's voice said, it's me, it's March the eleventh. Five words, and I pressed stop with my thumb, without looking up. I had the gesture down cold. You have things down cold that you've watched your whole life. I have had years to figure out whose voice that was, and what I've decided is not to. So when I tell you my position on the tape was principle, understand that it was principle the way a seawall is scenery.

In May, Marisol called. The buyers wanted to close on June twentieth, and nothing closes without two signatures, and the machine was holding the house hostage, or we were.

We met at the house on a Sunday. The rooms were empty by then, and empty rooms are loud; every word came back off the terrazzo. We set up two folding chairs and put the box between us and went through it item by item, like an exchange of prisoners. The deed copies to Dee. The negatives to me. The rings we did one each, no discussion, her hand and then my hand into the soap dish, and that was the first thing in six years we did at the same speed.

Then only the machine was left. Dee picked it up, still in its bag, and turned it over once, and didn't say anything about the tape. What she said was, she wrapped my retainer like this. Nineteen eighty-five. Wax paper, then the bread bag, then the rubber band, because I kept leaving it on my lunch tray at Carver. She held the machine the way you hold a thing somebody wrapped for you. I had spent two months ready to argue with her about privacy, and I had no argument ready for a retainer.

So we voted. Two to nothing, the only unanimous vote of the whole spring. The tape does not go in the ground, and it does not go to Boca. It stays in the machine, the machine stays in the box, the box stays packed, and if either of us ever plays it, we play it together or not at all. Probably not at all. I want to be honest. Probably not at all.

We buried her on the first Saturday in June, the two of us and the man from the gardens, at nine in the morning before the heat got serious. The niche took the urn and nothing else. Dee read the dates off the little plaque twice, quietly, like she was checking arithmetic.

The house closed on the twentieth, at six hundred five thousand, signed in two states.

The box lives on the top shelf of my closet in Boynton now. Before Dee flew home she stood at my kitchen counter and asked me to show her how Mom folded the bag, and I showed her. You fold the top over twice, you band the cord along the side, never across the front, so it can't dent the little tape door. She did it over until she had it. It takes four minutes to pack that box. We made coffee and it took us an hour.

Hurricane season runs June through November. Dee booked her flight back for the second of December, to help me take the box down, which has never in its life been a two-person job.

I told her to come anyway.

If the water rises before then, I take the box and I go. That part hasn't changed. But the first call I make now is Charlotte, and that part has.
