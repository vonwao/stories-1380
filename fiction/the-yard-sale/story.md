# The Yard Sale

I have worked yard sales on the Treasure Coast for thirty years, both sides of the table, and I can usually tell you what's wrong with one from the curb. Overpriced Pyrex. A reseller seeding the dollar box. The sale across the street took me three Saturdays, and I still got it wrong.

The new people moved into the Hendersons' old place in March. Nobody saw a moving truck. A week later the tables went up at eight in the morning, sharp, and the sign said yard sale, everything five dollars. Then the same tables the next Saturday, and the next.

Nobody holds a yard sale every Saturday. You run out of garage. And nobody prices everything at five dollars, nothing over, nothing under, and turns down haggling with a smile. I tested that. I haggle at the church bake sale.

So I went over to figure out the scam. I want credit for that. I crossed the street as a detective.

They were a couple about my age, maybe older. The woman wore a sun hat and sat with the cash box, and as I came up the driveway she said, "Morning, Carol," and I had not told her my name. I let it go, the way you do when you want to keep watching.

The tables were ordinary. Crock pots, paperbacks gone soft, children's clothes folded in thirds. Clean, though, and none of it smelled like a garage.

While I pretended to read the paperbacks, a man I half knew from the Winn-Dixie carried a tape measure up to the woman and gave her a five. Then he worked an old worn key off his ring, laid it in her open hand, and said, out loud, like a man swearing something in court, "This is the key to my first truck. Nineteen seventy-two Ford. My dad and I rebuilt the carburetor the summer I turned sixteen."

"Thank you," she said. That was the whole ceremony.

He walked past me ten pounds lighter, and also like a man trying to remember whether he'd left the stove on.

That's the price. Everything is five dollars and one thing from your past, handed over and named out loud. "Name it as much as you like," she said, pleasant as a bank teller. "It goes either way." Name the key, lose the key. Name the summer, lose the summer.

I didn't buy anything that first day. Detectives don't buy.

The next Saturday I found my garden gloves on the third table. Not gloves like mine. Mine. Left thumb mended with embroidery floss in the wrong green, because the night I mended them, two years ago, I was out of the right green. Except this pair was nearly worn through, palms gone shiny, a season of work in them that hasn't happened yet. I walked home and took my own pair off the nail in the garage. My mend, twice, years apart.

I went back and bought the worn ones. Five dollars, and, from the shoebox I keep on the closet shelf, my father's brass lighter. I named it. "My father's lighter. He let me light his cigarette with it at my graduation." The woman thanked me. Crossing back, I could remember owning a lighter the way you remember a story someone told you at a party. Pleasant. Secondhand. No smoke in it.

I would like to say that scared me off. It settled the question instead. A scam takes your money and leaves you embarrassed. This took exactly what it said it would, and gave me gloves I am apparently going to wear the hell out of.

So I kept going back. There was something of mine on those tables every Saturday, in among everybody else's somethings. Reading glasses in my exact case, the prescription heavier than mine is now. A hairbrush strung with hair whiter than mine has gotten around to being. Some of it I bought. The glasses I left where they lay. There is knowing, and there is wearing it.

And every Saturday, I paid. The key to the house on Naranja Avenue, three bedrooms, where I raised my daughter. A church program from Saint Lucie's, nineteen ninety-eight, a girl in a white dress on the cover. A wedding invitation, heavy cream paper, June. I named them small. A key. A program. An invitation. The woman never pushed.

You can drive past a house you lived in for nineteen years and feel nothing, it turns out. I slowed down on Naranja in April and couldn't think why my foot had come off the gas.

June used to be a hard month for me. This June I repainted the bathroom. It was just a month.

I should tell you about Tessa while I still can.

My daughter married a man I didn't think much of, nine years ago, in June, on the water in Stuart, and at the rehearsal dinner I said a thing. It was about the baby coming, and the math of the dates, and I said it loud enough for the groom's whole family. Tessa wrote me a letter with what I said quoted word for word, because she wanted me to have it in writing. The letter ends with the words don't call. I have kept it nine years in the shoebox, and until this spring I could have recited it with my eyes closed.

She used to say, back when she still said things to me: "You don't apologize, Mom. You redecorate."

In May, on the table with the children's clothes, I found a knitted cap. Yellow, child-sized, a dropped stitch at the crown. I drop that stitch. Forty years of knitting and I drop it in the same spot every time. I stood there holding a cap I have not knitted yet, for a child I have never been allowed to meet, and I bought it to send to her. Five dollars. And from the shoebox, a baby tooth in a film canister. Tessa's. Her first. I named it: "A tooth."

She looked at me a moment longer than usual. "You're sure," she said.

"It's a tooth," I said.

I mailed the cap to the address in Atlanta I am not supposed to use. No note. If you had asked me that afternoon about her baby teeth, I would have told you, honestly, that I was never the kind of mother who kept things like that.

The second Saturday in June, there was a photograph in a dime-store frame. An old woman on a porch I don't own, jalousie windows, white hair, the hairbrush hair. Laughing. Beside her, a woman with gray coming in and Tessa's exact slouch, and a girl of nine or ten holding a small yellow cap up to the camera, the way you hold up a thing that has been kept.

Five dollars. I had the five.

And one thing from my past. I opened the shoebox right there, and it was down to one thing. The letter. Ten Saturdays, and everything else from those years was already gone across that table — the key, the program, the invitation, the tooth, the lighter — every piece named small and handed over by me, on purpose, while I told myself, and told you, that I was investigating.

I had not been investigating. I had not even been buying my future. I had been selling off the evidence, Saturday by Saturday, and the lightness I kept walking home with was the point. The woman across the street never lied to me once. I did the lying. I named the price small so I could pay it.

Here is what was left, on a lawn in Port St. Lucie at nine in the morning. I cannot tell you anymore what I said at that dinner. I have the shape of it. The heat of it coming out of my mouth, the sound of forks stopping. The words themselves have gone faint, like a story somebody told me about a woman she used to know. The only place on earth where that sentence still exists, word for word, is a letter in my daughter's handwriting, and I was standing there with it in my hands, pricing it.

Hand it over, and I would be done. A clean woman with a photograph of a porch, and no idea what she ever did to be kept off it.

You cannot be sorry for a thing you can't remember. You can only be innocent of it. And innocent was never on offer.

"Just the photograph today?" the woman said. Kind as ever. I want that on the record: whatever they are, they never once misstated the price.

"Not today," I said, and set the frame down.

"We're here every Saturday, Carol," she said. "There's no rush."

I walked home slow. I sat at the kitchen table and read the letter through twice, out loud. Every word I said, in her handwriting. It was worse than the shape I had kept. Good. I needed it to be worse, because it was worse, and I am done rounding it down.

Tessa's number hasn't changed. I have dialed it and hung up enough times to keep it tested.

Four in the afternoon, the tables still out across the street in the heat. I dialed and let it ring. Two rings. Three.

I still had the five dollars in my hand.

Four rings, and then her voice, older and careful, saying hello like a question.

"Tessa," I said. "It's your mother. Don't hang up. I'm calling to tell you I remember exactly what I said, and I have it in front of me, and I'm going to say it back, and then you can do whatever you want."

There was a long quiet on the line. She did not hang up.

I unfolded the letter. I read her every word.
