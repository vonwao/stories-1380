# Third Wednesday

I am fifty-six years old, and a Broward County judge sent me to anger management because I put a paver stone through the windshield of my own truck. I did it in a customer's driveway in Coral Springs, in front of her and her two kids, in the middle of an invoice dispute I was winning. The stone was handy because I lay tile and pavers for a living. The windshield was mine because at the last second some part of me steered, and I've thought a lot about that part since. It had half a second to work with, and it picked the one thing on that driveway that only hurt me. The charge was disorderly conduct. The sentence was twenty-six weeks, Wednesday nights at seven, in a rented room in a strip mall on Rock Island Road in Margate, between a nail supply wholesaler and a Jamaican takeout that starts frying around six, so that everything I learned about my temper smells faintly of oxtail.

There were eleven of us, plus Phil, the counselor, a tired man with a workbook. Triggers, time-outs, I-statements. Phil read it to us like a man reading a furnace manual to people whose houses were already on fire. We sat on folding chairs borrowed from a church and never returned, the metal kind that bite the backs of your legs, and drank coffee from a machine that burned every pot by seven-fifteen. Hector hangs drywall and hadn't spoken to his brother in nine years. Junie keyed her ex-husband's truck down to the primer, both sides, and would tell you the truck had it coming. Terry I never did learn much about. And there was Doreen.

Doreen is around seventy. She worked thirty years in a school cafeteria in Margate, she wears the big wraparound glasses you get after cataract surgery, and her paperwork said disorderly conduct, which, looking at her, had to be a clerical error. Nobody asked. For eighteen weeks she said almost nothing, and I had her filed under harmless, the way I had everybody in that room filed under something, so I wouldn't have to look at them.

Week nineteen, the air conditioning died. Phil gave up on the workbook around eight, and we sat there sweating under the cheap light, and Doreen said, "I know a story," the way you'd offer somebody gum.

She told us about a town where every grudge you held, you carried. Physically. In bags. Old grudges weighed more, not less. One man's bags filled his whole garage, so that he slept in the yard, and when he died and his family opened the bags, there was nothing in any of them but a smell, like rain hitting a hot sidewalk, and that's how they knew whose grudges they had been. That is not how she told it. She told it better, slower, but I'm not going to try to do her voice, because half of what made it work was that she didn't perform it. She said impossible things in the same voice a cafeteria lady uses to tell you they're out of pizza.

I interrupted twice. Once to be funny about my ex-wife's luggage, once because the story was doing something in my chest I didn't care for, and a joke is a way of standing up and leaving without leaving. Hector didn't interrupt. Hector sat dead still with his forearms on his knees, all the way to the end.

That Saturday his brother called him. Nine years, and the man calls about nothing, a fishing report, and they talked for an hour. Hector told us this at the next class with his voice shaking, and he swears that when he picked up the phone he smelled rain on concrete. It was not raining. I'll say what I actually believe, which is that I don't know. A brother can just call. Coincidence covers a lot of ground in Florida. But it kept happening, and it only ever happened one way.

Junie got the next one. Doreen told a story about an island where it rained on each person separately, little private weather, and only on the ones who needed to cry and wouldn't, and the rain followed them indoors, into banks, into bed, until they quit fighting it. Junie listened to the whole thing without one word, which for Junie is an athletic event. Two days later she was at the light on Banks Road and started crying, big stupid tears, she said, like a sprinkler you couldn't shut off, and when it stopped, eleven years of being furious at that man had gone down maybe one inch. Her words. But it was the first inch, and she'd been at it eleven years.

You see the rule already, because you're hearing this all at once. It took us months. The stories came true, slightly, sideways, and only for whoever had listened all the way through without interrupting. Doreen never said that. She has never claimed one thing in that room. Junie asked her straight out what the stories were, and Doreen said they were just stories she'd been carrying, and somebody had to put them down somewhere. That is the most she has ever said about it.

Phil signed our certificates in March. Twenty-six weeks, complete. The county considers my anger managed, and I have the paper, suitable for framing. And then April had a third Wednesday in it, and nine of us showed up anyway, nobody having exactly agreed to. The landlord runs the nail supply place and lets us keep the room for forty dollars a month, cash, in an envelope Junie decorates with stickers. The coffee situation changed shape: now it's a pump thermos of Bustelo that Hector's wife makes, styrofoam cups, powdered creamer in a paper bag. We unstack the chairs. Doreen tells one story a month. That's the whole institution.

Terry quit, and I want to be fair to Terry. He did his twenty-six weeks. He said he wasn't going to spend his Wednesdays getting his insides rearranged by a retired cafeteria lady in a strip mall, and that he felt fine, and that's a sane position. Nobody chased him to the parking lot. Leaving is reasonable. I would have left too, except by then I'd noticed the price of staying, and I can't walk away from a thing once I know what it costs.

Because listening is not free. That's the part I'd want somebody to tell me. The stories get in under your shirt. Sitting all the way through one means sitting still while it finds the thing you came in carrying, and your whole body wants to crack a joke, correct a detail, anything to stand up inside yourself and walk out. For about a year I couldn't do it. I interrupted like a reflex, because it was one, and the stories never came true for me, not slightly, not sideways, and I told myself I didn't believe in any of it, the way you tell yourself things at two in the morning.

Then in October she told the one about the chair.

A country, she said, where once a year every person had to sit in a chair in the middle of town and hear, out loud, the kindest true thing anyone had ever known about them. Not flattery. And it was unbearable. People fled the country. People confessed to crimes to get out of it. Because you can armor yourself against an insult but not against being seen, and the ones who stayed in the chair, who took it all the way to the end, walked off different. Not better. Different. Like a field after rain.

I sat through it. All of it. My jaw ached from holding it shut. What it stirred up was my daughter, Casey, twenty-three then, who had not called me since the driveway, because her mother told her what I did, as she should have. The whole story long, I sat on that folding chair in the smell of oxtail and acetone and thought about my girl, and I did not make one joke, and it cost me what it cost.

Eleven days later, Casey called. About her water heater, officially. I drove over. I fixed it, the thermocouple, twenty minutes. And at the door, leaving, she said, "You always showed up. Whatever else. When something broke, you always showed up."

That was the kindest true thing.

I stood on her step and let her say it, all the way to the end, and I did not deflect it, and I did not improve on it. I just took it. It was unbearable, exactly the way Doreen said the chair was.

I drove home slow, in the rain, with the windows cracked.

So that's what we are. Nine people in a rented room on the third Wednesday of every month. I still don't know what the stories are. I don't know if Doreen is doing something, or if listening is the whole machine and she just found a way to make a roomful of angry people do it. Some Wednesdays I'm sure it's her. Some Wednesdays I'm sure it's only us, getting quiet together, and the quiet doing what quiet does. And I notice the second answer is not actually smaller than the first.

I haven't decided. I keep coming back partly so I don't have to.

Tonight is a third Wednesday. The takeout is frying. Junie is setting out the cups. Hector's wife sent the thermos. Doreen comes in last, with her purse on her arm, and settles into her chair like a bird coming down onto a wire, and looks around the room, the way she does, checking who's here.

"I know a story," she says.

I have nowhere to be. I fold my hands, and I keep my mouth shut, and I stay.
