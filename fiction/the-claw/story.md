# The Claw

I do inventory for a living, which is a way of saying I notice what's supposed to be there. The Publix on Jog Road in Greenacres has four amusement machines on the vendor contract: two gumball towers, a sticker machine, and a rocket ride that hasn't rocketed since two thousand nineteen. A company out of Tampa empties the quarters monthly and leaves a receipt. I file the receipt.

The claw machine showed up on a Tuesday in March, between the cart corral and the propane cage, and it was not on the contract.

No asset tag, no service sticker, no number to call when it eats your money. Plugged into the lamppost outlet maintenance swears has been dead for years. Corporate had never heard of it. Neither had Tampa. The procedure is a removal form and a forty-dollar haul fee, and I told myself it was the form.

For the first week nobody played it. Claw machines are stocked to look winnable, all plush. This one was stocked like a drawer in your mother's kitchen: a single glove, a retainer in a cloudy case, a dog tag with the name worn off, a green plastic dinosaur, a house key on a Marlins lanyard.

Then Reuben, who runs carts on weekday mornings, stopped in front of it and said, "That's my dinosaur."

He was nineteen and certain. He'd lost it the summer he was seven, the day he and his mom had one afternoon to clear out of the apartment on Purdy Lane. Same chewed tail. His quarters came straight back. Then he got quiet, took out his vape, and dropped it through the coin slot, which it should not have fit through. The machine lit up.

The claw on this machine does not go limp at the last second. It came down, closed around that dinosaur the way you'd pick up an egg, and set it in the chute. Set it, not dropped it. Reuben held it for a minute, then went and pushed carts for four hours. He never bought another vape, but I don't think the machine cares about lungs. That was just his price that morning. I never saw it take the same price twice.

I started a notebook, because that's what I do with anything that moves in and out of my building. A woman won her mother's thimble for a tube of lipstick. A kid won back his own retainer for his skateboard, the whole skateboard, through the coin slot.

Money came back every time, quarters, twenties, one man's hundred-dollar bill. The best I can say is that money is the one thing in your pocket you were already planning to give away. You'd think there'd have been lines. There weren't. Word moved quietly, and people came early and played alone.

Dot comes in Tuesdays for the buy-one-get-ones. Seventies, sharp, drives a Camry she probably shouldn't still be driving. In April she stopped at the glass and pointed at a thin gold ring in the second row. "That went down my kitchen drain on Swain Boulevard in nineteen eighty-six," she said. Her husband took the sink trap apart twice and never found it. Gone eleven years in August.

She went through her purse. Library card, returned. Scratch-off, returned. Then she went still in a way I didn't like, and took out men's drugstore reading glasses with a kink in one arm. I'd seen her in my checkout line, cleaning those glasses with the hem of her shirt, eleven years past anybody to hand them back to.

I heard myself say, "Miss Dot, don't." Telling a customer not to enjoy an amusement is not company policy.

She dropped them in, and the claw took the ring like it was built for exactly that, because as far as I can tell it was.

I said too much then. I called the machine a crook. A ring is a piece of metal; the glasses were the last thing on earth that touched his face every day.

Dot put the ring on. It went on the way rings go on in movies and never do at our age, easy, like the finger remembered. She looked at me the way you look at a cashier whose drawer is correct but whose math is wrong anyway.

"I know his face," she said. "I'll know it till they close the lid on me. What I'd forgotten was my own hand. I've been looking at a widow's hand for eleven years, and that was getting to be the whole truth, and now it isn't. You think it charged too much. It charged exactly. You keep a register; you know the difference."

I still think she overpaid. She still thinks I count wrong. We leave it there, most Tuesdays, and she waves at me with the ring hand.

Now the part I've been walking around. The prizes change overnight; nobody has ever seen it restocked. One morning in May there was a pocketknife in the third row. Cracked bone handle, brass gone brown, my grandfather's initials on the bolster. Nobody alive knows what I know about that knife.

My father gave it to me when I turned thirteen, like a promotion. His father had carried it before him. Two years later my parents came apart, and one night I heard my father on the phone saying things about my mother that I was not supposed to hear. I was fifteen. I wanted to take something from him that he could never get back. So I rode my bike to the C-Fifty-One canal off Sixth Avenue, threw the knife as far as I could, watched the water go flat, rode home, and said nothing.

That's not the bad part. The bad part is that my father decided he'd done it. He'd borrowed the knife that spring to cut a stuck line on the boat, and convinced himself he'd left it on a seawall. My father, who did not apologize, went through tackle boxes on his knees and apologized to his fifteen-year-old son. I let him. That Christmas he bought me a Buck with a belt clip, better in every way you can measure, and I have clipped it on every working day for twenty-one years, and it has never once been just a knife.

I didn't play right away. I watched the knife sit in that glass for three weeks, and I understand now why some people stand at it a long time and walk away for good. Anyone can win. It's that the machine makes you say to yourself, in a grocery store parking lot, what the thing you want is actually worth. Most of what I love, I have never had to price.

One morning before open I tried quarters, as a joke. They came back. I tried the twenty-year service pin off my vest, and I want it noted that I meant it. The pin came back at the same speed. Not insulted, not moved. Exact.

Then in June my father called about nothing, a fishing report, and halfway through he said, "You still got that Buck on you?" The way he always asks. I said yes, the way I always say. Afterward I sat doing math I'd been refusing to do. The Buck was bought with his apology. The apology was bought with a lie. The lie was mine, and I had been carrying all three on my belt like I'd earned them.

The next morning I went out before open. The lot was empty and pink. I unclipped the Buck, sheath and all, twenty-one years worn into the leather, and held it the way you hold a thing you are about to not own. Then I dropped it through the slot, and it didn't rattle into any coin box. It just went.

The claw came down slow. I won't dress this up. It was a claw machine doing what claw machines do. It closed around my grandfather's knife like a hand that had been waiting, carried it across, and set it in the chute. The flap door was warm, Florida-in-June warm, which is all that was. The crack in the bone was the same crack. It smelled, faintly, of canal.

I drove to my father's place in Loxahatchee that afternoon. He's seventy-one, still has the boat. I put the knife on the kitchen table between us and started with the canal, and then, because the knife was only half of what I owed, I told him about the phone call, and what the throwing had been for.

He opened the blade and looked at it a long time. His thumb found the crack without looking, the same way mine does. "I tore that garage apart," is what he finally said. Not, how could you. An inventory man's complaint. I come by it honest.

We did not repair twenty-one years in one afternoon. Some of his quiet had an edge, and I sat in it without trying to make it nice. That was the part of the bill I had to pay in person. But when I got up to go, he walked me to the truck, which he does not do, and said, "Bring it Sunday. We'll put a real edge on it."

The machine is still out there between the corral and the propane cage. I never filed the form. Some mornings somebody is already at the glass, doing their arithmetic. I don't tell them it's rigged, because it isn't. I don't tell them it's fair, because I'm still not sure fair is the comfort everybody assumes. I make sure the corral is clear, and I leave them to it.

My grandfather's knife is in my right pocket now. The pocket sits flatter than it has in twenty-one years, and I notice the missing weight forty times a day.

Sunday we put an edge on it. It cuts fine.
